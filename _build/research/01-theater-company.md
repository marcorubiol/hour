# Profile 01 — Small-medium theater / dance / new-circus company

A 3-15 person structure headquartered in Catalonia or Spain, touring Europe with anywhere between two and six works in active rotation. Examples used as live references in this document: **La Veronal** (contemporary dance, Marcos Morau, Barcelona; has toured more than thirty countries including Chaillot, Venice Biennale, Avignon, Tanz im August, Sadler's Wells), **La Calòrica** (text theatre, Barcelona, founded 2010; mid-sized touring inside Catalonia through Teatre Nacional, Poliorama, regional public theatres), and new-circus companies in the Manolo Alcántara / Eia orbit. These are not interchangeable — La Veronal plays international dance houses with 800-seat stages, La Calòrica plays the Catalan public-theatre circuit in Catalan. Hour has to hold both as the same profile because their workflow shape is the same even when the scale and currency differ.

The unit they think in is the **show** (the piece, the creation). Everything hangs off it: the programmers who might buy it, the dates already in the calendar for it, the materials that represent it, the cachés it pulls in. A CRM organised around "clients" is already the wrong abstraction — the client is a downstream property of the show-date relation.

## 1. What they juggle at the same time

Typical snapshot of a company that has been running five or more years:

- **1-2 shows in active touring.** The current "big" piece (often the most recent premiere that still has two to three years of life in it) and one predecessor kept in repertoire because it pays the bills. La Calòrica's touring pattern is a useful reference — *De què parlem mentre no parlem de tota aquesta merda* toured thirteen Catalan cities after a sold-out run at the TNC, while earlier works (*Fairfly*, *Els Ocells*) remain in repertoire (source: [teatrebarcelona.com / La Calòrica](https://www.teatrebarcelona.com/la-calorica), [Teatre Poliorama](https://www.teatrepoliorama.com/ca/programacio/c/766-els-ocells.html)).
- **1 show in creation / pre-production.** Rehearsals, residencies, co-production negotiations. This is the financial black hole of the year. Residencies are typically hosted at Graner, Mercat de les Flors, Tantarantana, La Caldera, Temporada Alta, or French CDN / CCN structures.
- **0-2 dormant shows** kept alive "in the catalogue." A programmer calls asking about a piece from 2019 — the company has to be able to remount it within three or four weeks if the conditions are right. This is why materials for old shows have to stay accessible and not rot.
- **1 brand / 1 legal entity in most cases.** Larger or more prolific choreographers (La Veronal is an example, Marcos Morau also makes commissioned work for NDT, Opéra de Paris, Ballet de Lorraine) effectively run two parallel tracks: the company's own repertoire and the author's contracted work. For Hour's purposes that is still one organisation with multiple projects, not two organisations — the office infrastructure is shared.

Live contact volume, rough bracket (no hard public data; based on typical sector practice and membership sizes of ADGAE, IETM, CDAEC):

- 80-250 **programmers / curators** in a working address book: public theatres (Mercat de les Flors, TNC, Teatre Lliure, CDN, Chaillot, HAU, Kampnagel, Sadler's Wells), private venues, festivals, networks.
- 30-80 **venues** with concrete commercial relationships (past or in-negotiation gigs).
- 10-30 **festivals** that matter annually (Grec, Temporada Alta, Fira Tàrrega, Fira Mediterrània, Avignon, Impulstanz, Tanz im August, Bitef, Kunstenfestivaldesarts, Romaeuropa, FIBA, Santiago a Mil).
- 5-20 **press contacts** (critics, cultural journalists, specialised magazines).
- 5-15 **técnicos** (freelance pool of lighting, sound, stage managers, regidors).
- 2-10 **performers** on regular contracts, plus guest bodies for specific productions.
- 3-8 **institutional counterparts** (ICEC, INAEM, Acción Cultural Española, Institut Ramon Llull, Institut français, Pro Helvetia, Goethe-Institut).

Order-of-magnitude. No solid public survey data on the Spanish / Catalan sub-sector specifically.

## 2. Annual cycle

European seasons are September-to-June. The Spanish / Catalan season broadly aligns, but the Catalan festival calendar produces a very different rhythm from, say, a German Stadttheater's.

Anchor dates a Barcelona-based company watches (verified):

- **February · FETEN, Gijón.** European performing arts fair for children and families. Edition 35 was 22-27 February 2026, 86 shows, 82 companies, 200 passes ([asturias.com / festival-feten](https://asturias.com/festival-feten/), [gijon.es](https://www.gijon.es/es/eventos/feten)). Matters to family-programming companies; much less relevant for La Veronal-shaped work.
- **April-June · IETM plenary and satellite meetings.** Spring plenary rotates across Europe. Not a market per se, but the place where programmers form their image of a company.
- **July · Festival d'Avignon (IN) and Avignon OFF.** The OFF is the biggest performing arts market in France: 1,400+ companies across ~139 venues in July, 3,500+ programmers attending, venue-rental model (not curated) ([festivaloffavignon.com](https://www.festivaloffavignon.com/), [avignon-et-provence.com](https://www.avignon-et-provence.com/en/culture/avignon-festival)). A Catalan text company rarely goes to OFF because of the French-language barrier; dance and physical work do.
- **August · Tanzmesse, Düsseldorf (biennial, even years).** The dominant contemporary-dance trade fair: up to 1,500 exhibitors and visitors end of August, Agora / Talk & Connect / Performance Program ([tanzmesse.com](https://www.tanzmesse.com/en/)). 2026 edition is under a funding cloud — NRW ministry announced cancellation of funding for 2026; a replacement *internationales tanzmeeting* was being discussed in December 2025 ([Dance Context Webzine](https://www.dancecontextwebzine.com/columns/awkward-political-idea-lets-save-tanzmesse)). This matters: if Tanzmesse 2026 does not happen, a whole year of international-dance dealmaking rewires.
- **August · Edinburgh Fringe.** Mostly English-language; a small number of Catalan physical / dance / circus companies have used it as a showcase, but it is not a default.
- **September (early) · Fira Tàrrega.** The Catalan / Spanish street-arts and live-performance market. 2026 edition 10-13 September, 33 first-time companies, 10 international pieces ([firatarrega.cat](https://www.firatarrega.cat/actualitat/en_agenda/325/firatarrega-2026)). If the show can live outdoors or in a non-proscenium space, this is the key domestic fair.
- **October · Fira Mediterrània, Manresa.** Popular culture + world music + Mediterranean tradition, every year since 1998. 2026 edition is 15-18 October. Artist proposals open November 2025, close 22 January 2026, selections communicated by 30 June 2026 ([firamediterrania.cat](https://firamediterrania.cat/en/professionals/participation-rules)). For companies with folk, traditional, ritual, or musical cross-overs.
- **October-December · Temporada Alta, Girona / Salt.** European-reference festival, ~92 shows, 28 premieres, €3M budget for 2025, runs mid-September to 12 December across 24 venues including two Barcelona satellites (Mercat de les Flors, Teatre Romea) ([temporada-alta.com](https://temporada-alta.com/en/), [ARA](https://en.ara.cat/culture/high-season-2025-putin-s-trial-ostermeier-s-hamlet-and-six-clock-funeral_1_5480998.html)). A Catalan company's most important domestic exposure window.
- **November · CINARS Biennale, Montreal (biennial, even years).** 1,200+ professionals from 50+ countries, the North American gateway ([cinars.org](https://cinars.org/en/biennale)). For a Catalan company to go is a significant investment; usually underwritten by Institut Ramon Llull, ICEC, or Acción Cultural Española.
- **Rolling · Mercat de les Flors season openings.** The 2025-2026 season marks twenty years of the Mercat as a dance-dedicated structure ([mercatflors.cat](https://mercatflors.cat/en/schedule/season-2025-2026-presentation/), [dansacat.org](https://www.dansacat.org/es/presentacio-temporada-2025-2026-del-mercat-de-les-flors/)). Its seasonal announcement is a moment where Catalan dance companies see whether they are in or out of their house.

**Submission deadlines are the silent killers.** Public theatre seasons in France (CDN network) are typically programmed 14-24 months out. German Stadttheater programming is fixed by March-April for the September-start season. Spanish municipal programmes are more erratic but frequently go through public-tender mechanisms (*redes de circuitos* like Danza a Escena, Platea, Circuit de la Xarxa de Teatres Públics) with their own windows. Lead time from first contact with a programmer to gig has traditionally been cited as **12-24 months in theatre and dance** (anecdotal, from distributor blog posts and interviews); shorter (6-9 months) for festivals that programme closer to date; longer (24-36 months) for large co-production deals involving multiple houses.

## 3. Difusión / booking

The Catalan / Spanish sector uses **distribució** (CA) / **distribución** (ES) as the umbrella term for what Hour labels "difusión" — finding programmers, sending them the show, closing a date. "Difusión" in Spanish is more common in **music** workflow; in theatre it leans **distribución**. ADGAE (Asociación de Empresas de Distribución y Gestión de las Artes Escénicas) is the state-level body ([adgae.org](https://adgae.org/)). In Catalonia, CDAEC groups distributors specifically ([holacdaec.wixsite.com/cdaec](https://holacdaec.wixsite.com/cdaec)).

### How programmers are found

Ranked by what actually closes gigs in this sector (based on distributor interviews and ADGAE-published notebook *La distribución de las artes escénicas en España*, by Eva Moraga Guerrero, 2023, cited via [ADGAE](https://adgae.org/); claim flagged as drawn from a single sector reference):

1. **Programmer sees the show live.** This is the single most reliable trigger. Any marketing exists to get a programmer into a seat on a night where the show is running. Fira Tàrrega, Temporada Alta, Avignon OFF, Tanzmesse, CINARS all exist for this reason.
2. **Peer-programmer referral.** "X told me I should see your work." Networks like IETM and Circostrada exist partly to manufacture this.
3. **Repeat booking.** A venue that programmed the previous show buys the next one on trust. Very common.
4. **Warm cold email** — programmer met briefly at a fair, followed up with dossier + trailer. Conversion rates are low; nobody has published hard ratios for the sector. Anecdotal figures from distributors: 1 gig for every 20-40 serious outbound contacts for a new show without trajectory. Flagged as unverified.
5. **Open calls and networks** — Perform Europe (EU-funded), Danza a Escena (state call, €8,000 + VAT per performance ceiling for 2024 edition, tech spec excluded, [masescena.es](https://www.masescena.es/actualidad/danza-a-escena-2024-abre-la-convocatoria-para-companias/)), Platea (INAEM), Circuit de la Generalitat.

### The sales-cycle stages

There is no single canonical pipeline, but across distributor interviews and what can be reconstructed from sector blogs and ADGAE material, the stages look like:

1. First contact (email, fair meeting, phone intro via a peer).
2. Dossier sent (ES / EN / FR / CA versions as needed).
3. Trailer link shared (60-90 seconds, Vimeo password-protected or unlisted YouTube).
4. Full video shared (password-protected Vimeo, short window, watermarked).
5. Programmer visits a run (the decisive step).
6. **Letter of interest** / preliminary date hold.
7. Economic negotiation (caché, technical needs, hospitality).
8. **Contract** signed, advance invoiced (commonly 25-50% on signature).
9. **Avance técnico** / technical advance exchanged (usually T-3 months).
10. Logistics confirmed: flights, hotels, per diems, ground transport.
11. Gig: load-in, show(s), load-out.
12. Settlement / remaining invoice / post-gig follow-up.

### New show vs. show-with-history

A premiere without trajectory is sold on the strength of the company name, the dossier, and — crucially — the trailer. Selling a show that already has two or three years of touring history is materially different: the company can show a reel of previous venues, press clippings, photos from multiple stagings, and a list of known programmer peers who have already bought it. This is where "social proof" becomes structural and why **the archive around a show is a sales asset, not a nicety**.

## 4. Production, confirmed-to-show-day

Indicative timeline (sector-standard, not a single published source):

- **T-12 to T-6 months:** contract signed, advance invoiced.
- **T-3 months:** *avance técnico* exchanged — the venue's technical director and the company's *director técnic* align lighting plot, sound plot, staging, video, rigging. This is a high-friction moment: venues push back on rider items, companies push back on hang heights and sightlines.
- **T-1 month:** flights booked, hotels confirmed, ground transport arranged, per diems calculated, VAT and withholding paperwork aligned with the venue.
- **T-2 weeks:** hospitality rider reconfirmed. Company sends final arrival sheet (who arrives, when, flight numbers, dietary needs).
- **Load-in day:** crew arrives, typically 6-10 hours of set-up for a medium dance/theatre piece; more for large or technically complex work. Venue crew numbers defined in rider.
- **Show day(s):** warm-up, photo call if requested, performance, strike.
- **Get-out:** immediate teardown or overnight, depending on schedule. ATA carnet stamped on exit if applicable.

### Riders

Two documents, never one:

- **Technical rider** (*ficha técnica* ES / *fitxa tècnica* CA): lighting plot, sound plot, video plot, staging plan, stage dimensions minimum, rigging, power, get-in access, crew required from the venue, timeline ([artist rider context at ohmyears.org](https://www.ohmyears.org/articles/artist-rider-template)). Mark Morris Dance Group's public *Socrates* tech rider is a commonly cited format reference ([markmorrisdancegroup.org](https://markmorrisdancegroup.org/wp-content/uploads/2019_import/documents/Technical-Specs/MMDG-SOCRATES-SAMPLE-RIDER.pdf)).
- **Hospitality rider**: dressing rooms, food and drink, dietary restrictions, accommodation standard, transport, per diems.

Riders get **versioned** constantly. If the lighting designer leaves and is replaced by another, every piece of paper about the show changes. If the show's cast changes from six to five dancers, dressing room needs change. Version drift is endemic and is the source of most venue-company disputes.

### Per diems and cost references

- **Spain**: collective-agreement references exist (convenio del sector artístico), but in practice per diems are company-to-company negotiated, commonly in the €40-60 range per day for domestic gigs. No hard public rate for contemporary dance.
- **UK**: Equity agreements have historically been used as benchmarks for subsistence; specific figures depend on category. Flagged: not independently verified here.
- **France**: the *intermittents du spectacle* system is not a per-diem framework but an unemployment-insurance annex (annexes 8 and 10) requiring 507 hours of work over 12 months to qualify ([francetravail.fr](https://www.francetravail.fr/spectacle/spectacle--intermittents.html), [Wikipedia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermittent_du_spectacle), [cheque-intermittents.com](https://www.cheque-intermittents.com/les-avantages-sociaux-des-intermittents-du-spectacle/)). It affects how French venues and CDN structures **classify and pay** visiting crew and performers when they are French residents, and what French collaborators need to accumulate.

### Transport

Medium shows: a van or small truck (furgoneta or camió). Larger shows: 12-tonne truck or a dedicated transport company (companies like Cargo Connection, Trans Europa, Nüssli are sector references). For **non-EU destinations — UK, Switzerland, Norway, USA, Canada** — the company needs an **ATA carnet** (*Admission Temporaire / Temporary Admission*): a "passport for goods" allowing temporary import / re-export of professional equipment without duties or VAT. Covers musical instruments, lighting, costumes, rigging, video, set. UK-issued rock-band carnet for EU+Scandinavia around £350 for £20,000 of gear as a common reference point ([livemusicbusiness.com](https://livemusicbusiness.com/for-music-artists-what-is-a-carnet/), [Wikipedia ATA Carnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ATA_Carnet), [UK.gov touring guidance](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/working-performing-and-touring-in-europe-guidance-for-the-performing-arts-theatre-opera-dance-circus-and-other-live-performance-genre)). Stamping happens at every border crossing — a missed stamp kills the carnet and triggers deposit claims.

### Roles

- **Dirección / coreografía**: creative lead, often non-commercial-facing.
- **Producción / gerencia**: single most overloaded role in a small company. Handles contracts, invoices, logistics, sometimes distribución too.
- **Distribución** (internal or outsourced): booking.
- **Dirección técnica**: owns the rider and the *avance técnico*.
- **Regidor / stage manager**: runs the show.
- **Performers**: 2-8 for most medium work.
- **Assistants / auxiliares**: per-project hires.

Smaller structures collapse multiple roles into one person (the producer is the driver is the stage manager). Larger structures split them properly.

## 5. Archive and materials

Per active show, the canonical asset list:

- **Dossier** in 3-4 languages (ES, CA, EN, FR — German less common but growing).
- **HD photographs**: press selection (5-10 hero images) plus full production stills (30-100+).
- **Full video**: single fixed-camera or multi-camera edit, typically password-protected Vimeo, 40-120 minutes.
- **Teaser / trailer**: 60-90 seconds, public Vimeo or Youtube, embeddable.
- **Technical rider** (PDF, versioned).
- **Hospitality rider** (PDF, versioned).
- **Ficha artística / artistic credits sheet**.
- **Lighting plot** (CAD / Vectorworks).
- **Sound plot**.
- **Video plot** (if applicable).
- **Staging plan** / floor plan.
- **Contract template** (aligned with ADGAE reference models and — for Catalan theatre — CDAEC conventions).
- **Press pack**: clippings, reviews, critics' quotes in multiple languages.

Formats drift: a lighting plot from 2019 made in Vectorworks 2019 opens weirdly in Vectorworks 2024. Dossiers exported as PDF with Word comments still visible is a common embarrassment. A rider from three tours ago still in circulation with the wrong tech contact is a known source of venue anger.

"Send me the rider" and "send me the photos" are the two most frequent incoming requests after a date is agreed. A company that can answer them in two minutes looks professional; a company that takes two days to find the right version looks amateur. **Versioned, dated, linkable asset bundles per show** are the product need here.

## 6. Money

### Structures

Most Catalan / Spanish companies exist as one of:

- **Sociedad Limitada (SL)** — most common for mid-scale, grown-up companies.
- **Cooperativa** — increasingly common, especially in Catalonia (cooperativisme).
- **Asociación cultural** — common for smaller or project-based.
- **Autónomo** individuals acting as the administrative head, contracting the rest as individual autónomos.

### Cachés (indicative ranges, flagged as sector estimate)

No reliable public single source; the figures below are composites from Danza a Escena caps, published programme budgets, and distributor blog commentary. Treat as order-of-magnitude:

- **Small-scale theatre / dance piece** (1-3 performers, minimal tech): €1,500-4,000 per performance.
- **Mid-scale** (4-7 performers, full tech rider, own crew): €5,000-15,000.
- **Large-scale** or internationally established (La Veronal tier): €20,000-50,000+.
- **Danza a Escena 2024** caps the cachet at **€8,000 + IVA per performance**, tech spec excluded ([masescena.es](https://www.masescena.es/actualidad/danza-a-escena-2024-abre-la-convocatoria-para-companias/)). That is a ceiling, not a floor.

### Agent / distributor commissions

Typical range cited across ADGAE material and distributor interviews: **15-20%** of caché for EU distribution. 10% is considered very cheap; 25% only in commission-plus-retainer mixed models. Flagged as sector-anecdotal.

### Tax and social regimes

- **Spain — IRPF retenciones on artistic activity.** Under the Estatuto del Artista (Real Decreto 31/2023, 24 January 2023), retenciones for artists were reduced from 15%: to **2%** for *relación laboral especial*, and **7%** for autónomos earning under €15,000/year in artistic activity where that activity is >75% of total income ([boe.es](https://www.boe.es/diario_boe/txt.php?id=BOE-A-2023-2023), [TaxDown](https://taxdown.es/blog/reduccion-irpf-artistas), [Cuatrecasas](https://www.cuatrecasas.com/es/spain/fiscalidad/art/modificaciones-tipo-retencion-para-artistas)). Standard IRPF retención for other artistic-activity autónomos remains **15%**.
- **Spain — IVA**: standard 21% on performance invoices to venues unless reverse-charge or exemption applies. Intra-EU B2B invoices often use reverse charge — requires valid VAT-registered counterpart.
- **France — intermittents du spectacle** (annexes 8 and 10 of assurance chômage): French venues hiring French collaborators will route them through this system; 507 hours in 12 months required to qualify ([francetravail.fr](https://www.francetravail.fr/spectacle/spectacle--intermittents.html), [etreintermittent.com](https://www.etreintermittent.com/le-statut-dintermittent-du-spectacle/), [CND FAQ](https://www.cnd.fr/fr/page/3783-faq-l-intermittence-du-spectacle)).
- **Germany — KSK (Künstlersozialkasse)**: artists based in Germany with ≥25% of work performed there can be insured through KSK. Minimum annual income threshold ~€3,900. For foreign performers paid by a German organiser for work **in Germany**, the organiser must report and pay **Künstlersozialabgabe (KSA)** contributions regardless of whether the performer is a KSK member ([touring-artists.info](https://www.touring-artists.info/en/social-security/users-contribution-social-security-contributions-for-artists), [kuenstlersozialkasse.de](https://www.kuenstlersozialkasse.de/)).
- **SGAE / AIE** (ES) — rights collection for authors and performers. Relevant when a show includes authored music; handled out-of-band in most cases but tracked.

### Payment delays

The sector-standard perception — public theatres in Spain pay slowly (60-90 days post-invoice, sometimes longer), French CDNs pay faster (30-60 days), festivals and private venues vary — is widely cited in distributor blogs and was institutionalised as a crisis during the 2009 recession ([Critical Stages](https://www.critical-stages.org/8/the-budget-is-the-thing-spanish-theatre-in-recession-spain/)). Exact 2026 figures per venue class: **no solid public data**. This is the kind of operational fact a multi-tenant tool like Hour could actually begin to measure if users consented.

Advances of 25-50% at contract signature are standard. Final payment on or after performance date, typically by transfer, 30-90 days later depending on the venue.

## 7. Tools used today and why they fail

Composite stack in an average Catalan company:

- **Gmail** or a domain-mapped Google Workspace inbox.
- **Google Sheets / Excel** for the tour tracker (dates, contacts, status, caché, advance paid y/n, invoice sent y/n, paid y/n).
- **Google Drive / Dropbox** for materials.
- **WhatsApp** groups for each tour.
- **Vimeo** for password-protected video.
- **Canva / InDesign** for dossiers.
- **Holded / Contasimple / Quipu** for invoicing and accounting (ES-specific).

Sector-specific tools that exist but are not widely adopted in Catalan / Spanish small-medium companies:

- **Artifax Event** — venue and event management software, UK-origin (1986), used by major venues (Wigmore Hall, Edinburgh International Festival) ([artifax.com](https://artifax.com)). Venue-side, not company-side.
- **Propared** — production planning for arts and events, schedules, budgets, contact lists, digital production books, templates for recurring shows ([propared.com](https://www.propared.com/)). Strong for the production layer, less for the booking funnel. North-American origin.
- **ArtsVision** — large-venue scheduling, mostly US.
- **Eventotron** — applications / submissions management, common at festivals.
- **QLab** — show-control software, used on-stage not in the office.
- **Momentum** — booking-agent CRM for music, not theatre.
- **IETM Satellite** — directory of the network, not a workflow tool.

Why the spreadsheet + Drive combo survives: it is the lowest-common-denominator tool both the company and any freelance distributor / técnic can open. Adoption of sector tools is blocked by price (Propared, Artifax are expensive for a 5-person company), by language (English-only UX), and by the fact that no single tool covers the **distribución → producción → archive → money** arc in one view. Companies end up with four tools that do not talk to each other, which is why the Excel remains the source of truth.

## 8. Collaboration patterns

The single most important human infrastructure question for this profile: **is distribución internal or outsourced?**

- **Internal**: the producer / gerent handles booking. Smaller budget, tighter message control, weaker international reach. Common in early-career and mid-scale companies.
- **Outsourced to a freelance distribuidor** who represents several companies in parallel. Higher reach, shared-calendar problems, conflict-of-interest dynamics between represented companies. In Catalonia: **Elclimamola** (Marine Budin + Marcela Imazio, specialised in contemporary scenic creation, [elclimamola.com](https://elclimamola.com/)), **Marta Oliveres** historically through Marta Oliveres Tortosa Management I Gestió Cultural SL (now extinguished as an entity, [empresite.eleconomista.es](https://empresite.eleconomista.es/MARTA-OLIVERES-TORTOSA-MANAGEMENT-GESTIO-CULTURAL.html); she remains active individually), the **CDAEC** network more broadly ([holacdaec.wixsite.com/cdaec](https://holacdaec.wixsite.com/cdaec)). Caterina Muñoz and Iaquinandi are further names cited in Catalan distributor circles (flagged: not all verified here as current operating entities).

The outsourced-distribuidor model creates a specific data-ownership problem: the company's Gmail has one thread with a programmer, the distribuidor's Gmail has another. When the contract arrives, is it the company who invoices or the distribuidor? (Usually the company, with commission paid out to the distribuidor after). When the programmer writes "I'm thinking about 2027" — who gets told?

The **técnic** pool is typically a rotating set of 5-15 freelance lighting designers, sound technicians, stage managers, regidors. Information flows from the company's dirección técnica to the freelance técnic via email + PDF rider. Freelances pool with 2-4 companies in parallel — they need to see their own schedule across all of them, which no company-side tool delivers.

Tension between in-house staff (producer, director) and freelance collaborators (técnics, guest performers, distribuidor) is a recurring theme: the in-house team has full context; the freelance sees only their slice. Shared-calendar accidents — a date confirmed verbally by the director but not entered in the shared calendar, then overwritten by the producer — produce the worst kind of friction.

| Lighting designer | iluminador / diseñador de luces | il·luminador / dissenyador de llums | lighting designer | éclairagiste / créateur lumière |
| Sound engineer / designer | técnico de sonido | tècnic de so | sound engineer / designer | ingénieur du son |

Nuance notes:

- **función / bolo / fecha** are not interchangeable in Spanish. *Función* is the artistic event on a given night. *Bolo* is that event considered as a paid job (slightly informal). *Fecha* is the slot in the calendar — abstract, pre-filled. A company might "tener 15 fechas firmadas" (15 dates signed), each of which is one or more funciones and one bolo.
- **distribución** is the theatre-sector default in Spanish and Catalan (distribució). **Difusión** in Spanish is music-industry language and broader outreach. Mixing them is a tell.
- **programador** is the person who curates a venue's or festival's season. "Promoter" does not carry over well to theatre — the word *promotor* in Spanish-speaking performing arts is rare and smells of concert-industry imports.
- **Catalan usage is distinctive**: "caixet" is heard for *caché*; "fitxa tècnica" for rider; "companyia" spelled with -y- not -ñ-. Catalan theatre culture uses "estrena" very precisely to mean the *opening night* of a new production, not just "release."
- **régisseur** in French is structurally different from the Spanish *regidor* — *régisseur général* is the production's overall technical-operations lead, *régisseur de scène* is closer to the Spanish regidor. Hour's vocabulary UX cannot translate these 1:1.

## 10. Friction and pain stories

Flagged explicitly: these are anecdotal, drawn from distributor blogs, sector forums, and widely-repeated workplace stories. Names are illustrative, not citations.

- **The 2-year-old rider.** A company agrees a gig at a French regional theatre. The production manager forwards "the rider" from the Drive folder. The document is two summers old; it lists the previous lighting designer as contact and uses a lighting plot that was changed after a revival. The venue's régisseur pre-orders fixtures against the old plot. Load-in morning: nothing fits. Four hours lost.
- **The Tanzmesse follow-up that never happened.** A dance company meets a German festival programmer on day two of Tanzmesse. Brilliant coffee meeting, programmer says "send me the full video, I am thinking about 2027." The company producer is buried in the rest of Tanzmesse, notes it on a napkin, the napkin is lost. Three months later the company realises and sends the email. The programmer has already filled 2027.
- **The double-booked week.** Director says yes to a private promoter on a phone call for a specific week. Producer simultaneously confirms a public-theatre date for the same week. Nobody updates the shared calendar. Two weeks before the first date, the conflict surfaces. One gig has to be cancelled with penalty.
- **The wrong performer on the flight.** Producer books a flight for the performer whose name is closest in the travel-booking form. It is the right first name, wrong last name — a different performer from a different show. Caught when the boarding pass arrives.
- **The French CDN invoice that aged four months.** A CDN pays reliably but only against a complete dossier: *attestation fiscale*, *attestation URSSAF*, VAT form, performer list with social numbers. One document missing → invoice parked. Nobody tells the company. Four months later, the producer chases. Paperwork was missing from day one.
- **The forwarded dossier with internal comments.** Producer exports dossier as PDF from a Word document. Track-changes comments from last month's internal review are still embedded. Dossier goes to three programmers. One replies amused; another notices and mentions it to a peer. Reputational damage is small but specific.
- **The lost email thread.** A six-month thread with a programmer — dossier, video, press, scheduling — lives in one producer's inbox. That producer leaves the company. The thread is technically inside the company's Google Workspace; nobody knows the subject line or the programmer's exact email. The relationship resets from zero.

Each of these is, in Hour's terms, a **system failure** — an event that should have had a durable object in the database and instead had a paper napkin.

## 11. What they do NOT want software to do

The artistic core is sacred. This profile does not want software to:

- Interpret the **rehearsal process**. Rehearsal is a creative space, deliberately non-transparent, deliberately not optimised. Any software that tracks "rehearsal productivity" is hostile to how the work is made.
- Generate or suggest **programming direction**. Curatorial choices are the artistic director's. A "recommended next show" feature is read as insulting.
- Automate **the relationship with a programmer**. Trust-building with a programmer happens over years of being seen, being remembered, being present at the right dinners. A CRM that auto-sends "it's been 90 days since your last contact with X, would you like to send a follow-up?" is actively embarrassing and damaging. The programmer can smell it.
- Replace the **curatorial conversation**. Pitching a show is not a funnel; it is a conversation about whether the work and the house fit. "Close the deal" UX is wrong.
- Enforce **consistency for its own sake**. Two shows in the same catalogue can legitimately have wildly different rider lengths, different dossier styles, different numbers of assets. Forcing them into a template denies the specificity of each work.
- **Quantify** artistic success. Seats sold ≠ artistic quality in this sector. KPIs on performances per year, venues per country, etc., are operational metrics at best — they should never be surfaced as an artistic judgement.

Where efficiency *is* a bug: in the relationship layer. The thing that closes a gig is a programmer having seen the company's work twice and remembered the conversation at Tanzmesse. Software that compresses that to a pipeline stage kills the thing it is trying to measure. Hour's right reflex is to make the operational layer invisible so the relational layer can breathe.
