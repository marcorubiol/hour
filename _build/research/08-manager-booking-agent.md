# Profile 08 — Manager / booking agent representing multiple artists

This is the music-industry analog of Profile 06 (freelance cultural distribution agent). The two profiles share a core structural fact: one person (or a 2-5 person shop) sells many acts in parallel, not one company selling its own shows. But the music operator diverges from the performing-arts distributor in three decisive ways:

1. **Roster is the organising object, not the show.** A distributor's week is organised by the next ten gig-leads for a specific show. A booking agent's week is organised per artist-in-roster, and every artist has several live threads active at the same time (tour, festival run, showcase).
2. **The work is career-wide, not campaign-wide.** A manager's remit is the next five years of an artist's career. A booker's remit is narrower (live only) but still structural — album cycles, not isolated gigs.
3. **Deeper artist-life involvement.** Managers book flights, resolve band conflicts, coordinate with labels and publishers, field personal requests. The file is not just "artist business" — it is the artist.

For Hour, this profile is the stress test for the multi-tenant assumption that "show-centric" is the right primary object. Music does not fit that assumption cleanly. The primary object here is the **artist/act** and the **tour** is a campaign spanning many "shows."

## 1. What they juggle

**Roster size.** Typical bands in this research:

- **Manager (career-wide):** 3-8 artists. 15-20% of gross across all artist income streams (live, recorded, sync, brand, publishing advances, merch). [MMF / Music Week](https://www.musicweek.com/management/read/as-deal-structures-evolve-mmf-publishes-guide-for-music-managers-on-new-commercial-models/090821); [SmartistU on artist management](https://smartistu.com/artist-management-contracts).
- **Booking agent (live only):** 10-25 artists. 10-15% of gross live fees in the US/UK, 15% common in Europe, sometimes up to 20% for emerging artists who need more per-gig work. [Harmony Central forum](https://www.harmonycentral.com/forums/topic/7143-what-percentage-do-booking-agents-get/); [rem.fan on booking agent earnings](https://rem.fan/how-much-do-booking-agents-make/); [Stagent "How to start a booking agency"](https://stagent.com/blog/how-to-start-a-booking-agency).
- **Combined manager-booker:** 3-10 artists. Common in Spain / Catalonia / Netherlands / Ireland for jazz, experimental and small indie, because budgets do not sustain separate roles. Matt Fripp (Jazzfuel) describes his own roster as "more than 40 artists for more than 2,000 club, venue and festival gigs in Europe, North America & Asia" over 15+ years, implying a rolling active roster in the low double digits. [Jazzfuel about](https://jazzfuel.com/about/). Danielle Oosterop (Amsterdam, improvised music) describes her role as covering "tour bookings, fundraising, logistics, visibility, and long-term career building" — a hybrid that is normative in that scene. [Improvised Music Company](https://www.improvisedmusic.ie/supporting-musicians/resources-opportunities/so-you-want-a-booking-agent-insights-from-danielle-oosterop).

**Per-artist parallel streams.** At any given week, each roster artist has several threads active at once:
- a **career plan** (where do we want this artist in 2 years)
- a **live campaign** (the current album tour, plus holds for next year's festivals)
- a **release campaign** (recorded music pipeline, label coordination, PR roll-out)
- a **press campaign** (interviews, showcases, playlist pitching)

These are not sequential. A manager may be pitching press on album 3 while negotiating the live contract for album 2's closing tour while scouting producers for album 4.

**Lifecycle heterogeneity.** The roster is rarely uniform: one artist just signed, one is in album-2 touring, one is fading and the conversation is "should we release a compilation and rest." **Dormant artists** are a real category — still on the roster, still entitled to inbound offers being processed, but no outbound work being done. Management contract sunset clauses (typical 3-5 years tapering) mean even artists who have left the roster stay as passive entries in the ledger for commission purposes. [Romano Law on manager contract terms](https://www.romanolaw.com/key-contract-terms-for-musicians-and-managers/); [Axis Legal Counsel on management agreements](https://axislc.com/public/top-legal-issues-and-deal-making-tips-for-negotiating-an-artist-management-agreement-for-musicians-singers-songwriters-music-bands-groups-and-recording-artists/).

**Contact density.** A mid-career booking agent's address book easily runs into the low thousands: promoters at every significant European club venue, festival talent buyers, local support acts and their agents, tour managers, press contacts per territory, playlist curators, sync agents, fellow booking agents in other territories, managers of other artists the roster might open for. [Jazzfuel on building scene knowledge](https://jazzfuel.com/jazz-booking-agent/). Bigger agencies routinely share contact databases across agents, but with ownership metadata (whose contact is this).

**Artist private life.** A manager is usually expected to handle flights, visa paperwork, hotel preferences, dietary restrictions, and emotional / logistical bandaids (a band member's partner is about to give birth; a soloist wants to skip a festival because of a mental-health dip). This is an explicit deliverable in Spanish and European small-management arrangements, not a sidecar. Oosterop and Fripp both frame this as normative rather than exceptional in the jazz/avant scene.

## 2. Annual cycle

European booking agents work against overlapping calendars. Core cycles:

**Festival announcement season.**
- **Summer festivals (European, June-September):** public announcement staged October to April, but the real booking conversations run **December to February** for headliners and **September to November** for early-bird emerging acts. Talent buyers build shortlists at showcase festivals (see below) and firm up bookings between those events.
- **Winter/spring indoor festivals:** programmed earlier in the preceding year.

My search for specific Spanish summer festival announcement timing returned mostly consumer-facing pages rather than industry-cycle documentation. The claim above is standard industry knowledge but is **weakly sourced** for Spain specifically — no industry-publication data point was located in this pass. [Music Festival Wizard Spain guide](https://www.musicfestivalwizard.com/festival-guide/spain/) shows confirmed lineups by early 2026 for mid-year festivals. **Flag as partial source.**

**Showcase festival circuit.** Four to six events per year dominate an agent's travel calendar:
- **Eurosonic Noorderslag** (Groningen, January) — 4,000+ delegates, 400+ festivals scouting. European primary. [Eurosonic profile via Initiative Musik](https://www.initiative-musik.de/en/showcase-festivals/).
- **Jazzahead!** (Bremen, April) — ~2,800 trade participants, the world's largest jazz industry meeting. [Jazzahead via WOMEX](https://www.womex.com/virtual/jazzahead_messe/news/jazzahead_is_calling).
- **Primavera Pro** (Barcelona, late May/June) — 5-day pro conference parallel to Primavera Sound. Key Iberian touchpoint. [Primavera Pro via Showcase Music links](https://www.showcase-music.com/showcase-music-links/).
- **Reeperbahn Festival** (Hamburg, September) — one of the largest club festivals in Europe with a significant industry program. [Reeperbahn festival](https://www.initiative-musik.de/en/showcase-festivals/).
- **WOMEX** (rotating European cities, October) — world music / global trade fair plus showcase. [WOMEX showcases](https://www.womex.com/programme/showcases).
- **ILMC / International Live Music Conference** (London, March) — not a showcase but the trade gathering covered by IQ Magazine. [IQ Magazine](https://www.iqmagazine.com/).

A booking agent with a European-jazz or indie roster will typically attend 3-5 of these every year. Travel, meetings (usually 20-60 per showcase, 15-30 min slots), showcase attendance and hospitality fill the full week on site. Agents report that showcase weeks are where the next 12-18 months of confirmed dates are seeded.

**Touring windows.**
- **Clubs / theatres:** autumn (September-November) and spring (February-May).
- **Festivals:** summer (June-early September), with peripheral events March-October.
- **Showcase-heavy months:** January (ESNS), April (Jazzahead), May-June (Primavera Pro), September (Reeperbahn), October (WOMEX).

**Spanish tax/accounting crunch.** For Spain-based agencies (autónomo or SL structures): quarterly IVA filings (Modelo 303) in January, April, July, October; annual summaries (Modelo 390) in January; personal income tax (renta, Modelo 100) April-June. IRPF withholding on artist invoices (7% reduced or 15% standard for ongoing activity) must match. [Autónomo Spain invoice requirements](https://www.autonomospain.com/invoice-requirements/); [Invoiceness 2025 Spain VAT guide](https://invoiceness.com/en/blog/do-i-need-to-add-vat-in-spain-2025).

**Artist release cycles.** Overlaid on all of this: each artist's album release is its own 12-18 month cycle (pre-release single drops, announce, release, tour, deluxe/EP, dormant, next record). The manager's work is synchronising live, press and release across the whole roster — agreeing a tour does not hit before the album drops because that would leak the material.

## 3. Difusión / booking

"The pitch is always the roster." A booking agent's inbound credibility is built on who else they already represent; a promoter who trusts one artist in the roster is easier to sell on a second. Matt Fripp (Jazzfuel): "booking agents often take on new artists via recommendations from people in their professional network." [Jazzfuel](https://jazzfuel.com/jazz-booking-agent/).

**Inbound vs outbound.** Unlike Profile 06 (freelance distributor cold-pitching shows), a known booking agent has a non-trivial inbound stream:
- Promoters emailing "is X available August weekend?"
- Festival talent buyers asking for holds on showcased artists.
- Fellow agents offering co-representation (their artist needs an opener in the agent's territory).

Outbound pitches still dominate volume (emerging artists, new releases, new territories), but the inbound/outbound split visibly improves with roster reputation. [Ludwig Sound](https://www.ludwigsound.com/) and [Annibale](https://www.annibale.eu/) are examples of European agencies whose roster visibility drives inbound.

**Offer cycle.**

1. **Initial contact / availability check** (email or phone): "Is X available for date Y? Ballpark fee Z?"
2. **Hold request.** Agent asks promoter to hold the date. Hold status is ranked: **1st hold** = first right of refusal, **2nd hold** = second, etc. Holds are typically valid up to 14 calendar days then released if not challenged or confirmed. [Thecurrent on booking mysteries](https://blog.thecurrent.org/2016/07/who-plays-where-when-and-why-explaining-the-mysteries-of-live-music-booking/); [The GRAND venue holds policy](https://www.thegrandyyc.ca/policies-holds-challenges); [Josiah Soren touring guide](https://www.josiahsoren.com/articles/a-comprehensive-guide-to-booking-your-own-tour).
3. **Offer.** Promoter issues offer sheet: fee (flat guarantee, or guarantee-vs-door split), capacity, ticket price, deductibles, curfew, support slot treatment. [Ticket Fairy on booking contracts & settlements](https://www.ticketfairy.com/blog/deal-or-no-deal-navigating-booking-contracts-settlements-for-venues-in-2026); [Tour Manager Info on settlements](https://tourmanager.info/show-settlement/).
4. **Counter / negotiation.** Agent replies, usually pushing on guarantee, walk-in time, soundcheck, accommodation, ground transport, hospitality rider compliance.
5. **Confirmation.** Verbal or email confirmation of terms. This is the status change that often gets missed in generic CRMs because it is not backed by a document yet.
6. **Contract.** Booking contract issued by agent (on agency template) or by promoter. Signed both sides.
7. **Advance / deposit.** Typically 50% of guarantee on contract signature. Final 50% on the day or 30 days post-show depending on market. [Ticket Fairy](https://www.ticketfairy.com/blog/deal-or-no-deal-navigating-booking-contracts-settlements-for-venues-in-2026).
8. **Advancing** (in the tour-production sense, not financial): 2-3 weeks before the date, the tour manager (or the agent, for small artists) reconfirms rider, tech needs, stage plot, day-sheet, access, hospitality.

**Conversion ratios.** Anecdotal from Jazzfuel and Oosterop: a known booking agent may see 50-80% confirmation on outbound pitches to warm contacts (existing promoter relationships, returning-artist context). Cold outbound to new promoters runs much lower (10-30%). Freelance distributors in Profile 06 typically report 10-20% overall — the manager-agent advantage is trust and roster breadth, not better emails. **Flag as anecdotal**; no aggregated industry study found.

**"I represent X" vs "I'm pitching X."** Important distinction. "I represent" = exclusive or primary booking agreement; agent speaks with authority on all fee/date/rider decisions. "I'm pitching" = either soft-representing (no exclusive) or operating as a secondary agent. Promoters read this framing carefully, and getting it wrong (overstating representation) is a reputational risk.

## 4. Production

**Booking agent ≠ tour manager.** The booker does not (usually) travel with the artist. The artist's tour manager (TM) runs day-to-day production: load-in, soundcheck, settlement, cash, hotels, van, driver. The booking agent holds the upstream layer: contract, advance payment, promoter contact, rider master, stage plot master, travel authorisations, and the financial chase post-show.

**The handoff.** Once a date is contract-confirmed:
- Agent sends TM the full advance pack (contract, promoter contact, venue sheet, hospitality rider, tech rider, stage plot, parking instructions, per diem amounts, emergency numbers).
- TM owns the day of show.
- Settlement sheet after the show comes back to the agent (or to the TM first, then to the agent for reconciliation).

**For small artists, the booking agent IS the tour manager by default.** Oosterop's description of "tour booking and production logistics" as a combined role is the default at jazz / avant / small-indie scale. The same person handles the offer and the parking instructions. This only separates at ~40-50 gigs/year or fee levels that justify a dedicated TM cost (€150-250/day plus travel).

**Documents the agent owns vs the TM owns.**
- **Agent:** contract, fee structure, invoice to promoter, deposit tracking, master rider (latest version), master stage plot, EPK, bio, promo photos.
- **TM:** day sheet, per-venue advance, cash envelopes, settlement docs, band logistics (hotel confirmations, van rental, fuel receipts), set list changes.

The **payment chase** stays with the agent: if the final 50% does not land 30-60 days after the show, it is the agent who escalates.

## 5. Archive and materials

Per artist, the file contains:

- **EPK** (electronic press kit) — one-page visual + bio + press quotes + video/audio links. Latest version is the canonical artefact. [About My Sound 2026 EPK checklist](https://aboutmysound.com/blog-posts/the-2026-musician-epk-checklist-what-booking-agents-actually-want-to-see).
- **Bio** — short (150 words) and long (400-600 words) versions. ES / EN bilingual for Iberian rosters; sometimes FR / DE for touring territories.
- **Hospitality rider** — food, drink, dressing room, ground transport.
- **Technical rider** — PA, monitors, backline, inputs, mic spec.
- **Stage plot** — visual of stage layout, input list, power needs. [Off Trail Studios on tech riders and stage plots](https://www.offtrailstudios.com/post/guide-to-tech-riders-and-stage-plots-for-shows-and-tours); [Tour Manager Info on stage plots and input lists](https://tourmanager.info/stage-plot-input-list/).
- **Promo photos** — hi-res, low-res, horizontal, vertical; photographer credit metadata matters for press.
- **Videos** — one featured live video, plus library of clips.
- **Press kit / press quotes** — assembled with sourced outlets.
- **Contract templates** — agency-standard plus artist-specific carve-outs.
- **Tax forms** — for US touring, W-8BEN; for intra-EU, VAT registration confirmations, IRPF retention documentation, NIF. [Touring Artists VAT guide](https://www.touring-artists.info/en/value-added-tax/vat-on-foreign-transactions).
- **Platform access** — Spotify for Artists, Apple Music for Artists, Bandcamp admin, YouTube artist channel, sometimes DistroKid/CD Baby.
- **Booking agent offer sheet template** — the agency's standard opening offer format.

**Multiply by roster size (8-25).** A booker with 15 artists holds 15 EPKs, 15 riders, 15 stage plots, 15 bios (× 2 languages × 2 lengths) and so on. Versioning compounds the problem: riders get updated after every lineup change or new backline requirement, and the wrong version going to a promoter is a chronic error category (see friction stories).

Hosting riders on a shared link so the "latest version" is always canonical is the documented best practice. [Off Trail Studios](https://www.offtrailstudios.com/post/guide-to-tech-riders-and-stage-plots-for-shows-and-tours).

## 6. Money

**Commission structures.**
- **Booking agent:** 10-15% of gross live fees (Europe standard 15%, up to 20% for emerging acts). [rem.fan](https://rem.fan/how-much-do-booking-agents-make/); [Vocalist entertainment agents](https://vocalist.org.uk/entertainment-agents).
- **Manager:** 15-20% of gross across all artist income. MMF default "around 20%" with sunset clauses. [Music Week on MMF guide](https://www.musicweek.com/management/read/as-deal-structures-evolve-mmf-publishes-guide-for-music-managers-on-new-commercial-models/090821).

**Invoicing patterns.** Two common models:
1. **Agent invoices promoter directly for full fee, then pays artist net of commission.** Agent carries float risk; artist paid faster. Preferred by artists, harder on agency cash flow.
2. **Promoter pays artist (or artist's company) directly; artist invoices agent or vice-versa for the commission.** Agency cash flow is cleaner; artist bears the chase risk.

Split invoicing (part fee, part buy-out, part merch) multiplies admin load.

**VAT complications (EU cross-border).** For B2B services between VAT-registered parties in different EU member states, the general rule is **reverse charge**: supplier issues invoice without VAT, buyer self-accounts. [Avalara reverse charge](https://www.avalara.com/us/en/vatlive/eu-vat-rules/eu-vat-returns/reverse-charge-on-eu-vat.html); [Marosa VAT Spain reverse charge](https://marosavat.com/manual/vat/spain/reverse-charge/); [Touring Artists VAT on foreign transactions](https://www.touring-artists.info/en/value-added-tax/vat-on-foreign-transactions).

For a Spanish autónomo agent invoicing a German promoter for agency services: no Spanish IVA charged, reverse charge note required, both VAT numbers on invoice. For the artist invoicing the promoter: different regime depending on whether the service is "performance" (often treated under special artist rules with local withholding) or agency-mediated. Each member state has idiosyncratic artist-tax withholding regimes (Germany's §50a, France's régime spécifique). **No single CRM handles all of this cleanly.**

Spanish autónomo-specific: IRPF retention (7% reduced for new-activity autónomos in the first three fiscal years, 15% thereafter) must be withheld by the Spanish business client on artist invoices issued in Spain. For non-Spanish clients, IRPF does not apply; but Spanish IVA exemptions for artistic services are narrow. [Autónomo Spain](https://www.autonomospain.com/invoice-requirements/); [Spanish.Tax IVA guide](https://spanish.tax/guides/iva-vat).

**Rights society passthroughs.** For recorded music played or for authored compositions performed live: SGAE (Spain), GEMA (Germany), PRS (UK), SACEM (France) all collect from promoters and venues separately and redistribute to registered rights-holders via reciprocal agreements. This is **not** revenue the booking agent controls, but it is revenue the artist expects to see, so the agent often fields questions about it. [PRS global partners](https://www.prsformusic.com/our-global-network/partners); [Wikipedia SGAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociedad_General_de_Autores_y_Editores); [Wikipedia GEMA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GEMA_(German_organization)).

**Deal terminology.**
- **Guarantee / flat fee** — fixed amount regardless of ticket sales. [Tour Manager Info settlement guide](https://tourmanager.info/show-settlement/).
- **Buy-out** — promoter pays flat and keeps all ticket revenue. Common for festivals.
- **Door deal / split** — artist takes a percentage of net door after agreed deductions.
- **Guarantee vs percentage** — artist takes the higher of a guaranteed floor or a % of net. Standard for headline club shows.
- **Deposit / anticipo / advance** — 50% on signature is typical; fully paid-out festivals day-of.

**Payment delays.**
- **Festivals:** mostly day-of (cash or wire on show day) or within 7 days.
- **Private clubs / promoters:** 30-60 days net is common, longer in southern Europe.
- **Public venues (municipal, theaters):** 60-90 days because of public-admin invoicing cycles.

No single industry source lists these windows authoritatively — data comes from scattered blog posts and agent interviews. **Flag as triangulated anecdote.**

## 7. Tools today and why they fail

The stack used by small European booking agents and managers, based on software review sites and industry commentary:

**General-purpose tools abused into booking use:**
- **Gmail** — per-artist inbox, per-agent inbox, or shared agency inbox (bookings@). Threading per artist per offer per tour date is manual.
- **Google Drive** — canonical file store for riders, contracts, photos. Versioning is human.
- **Google Calendar** — one calendar per artist, overlays for tour routing. Sync with bookings database is manual.
- **Airtable** — heavy use as roster DB, gig tracker, contact DB. Not built for holds/offers but flexible enough to script around. [Airtable article](https://www.airtable.com/articles/workflow-management).
- **Notion** — growing. Often used for internal agency docs, artist onboarding, tour prep.

**Booking-industry purpose-built tools:**
- **Muzeek** ([muzeek.com](https://muzeek.com/)) — claimed best-in-class for small agents / artists. Holds, confirmations, branded booking confirmations, settlements, integrated payments, team permissions, freemium pricing. [Muzeek help center](https://help.muzeek.com/en/articles/2205408-what-s-muzeek); [Capterra Muzeek](https://www.capterra.com/p/210127/Muzeek/).
- **Overture** ([bookingwithoverture.com](https://bookingwithoverture.com/)) — claims 55k agents/artists across 350+ agencies. Bookings, contracts, calendars, artist collaboration in one dashboard. Direct Muzeek competitor.
- **Stagent** ([stagent.com](https://stagent.com/)) — Netherlands-origin, gig/payment/calendar tracker. €39/month for solo agents, €99/month up to 10 artists, €799/month enterprise. Explicit pricing is rare in this segment and useful for Hour positioning.
- **Gigwell** ([gigwell.com](https://www.gigwell.com/)) — end-to-end: contracts, deals, logistics, invoices, Tour IQ (venue/festival database), ticket counts. Larger-agency oriented. [Gigwell vs Stagent](https://www.gigwell.com/blog/gigwell-vs-stagent); [Capterra Gigwell](https://www.capterra.com/p/162663/Gigwell/).
- **Prism.fm** — venue/promoter-side with some agent features. Lacks mobile + automated offer creation. [Gigwell vs Prism](https://www.gigwell.com/blog/gigwell-vs-prism).
- **Master Tour** — industry standard for larger tours. Not typical at 3-8 artist agency scale.
- **Backstage Hub, Eventotron, Rockhouse** — frequent mentions in festival-application and advance workflows.
- **SystemOne** ([systemonesoftware.com](https://www.systemonesoftware.com/)) — one platform for booking agencies, explicit manager/agent distinction in their content.

**The "holds" feature is the diagnostic.** Generic PM tools (Trello, Asana, Monday, Notion) do not model first hold / second hold / third hold / challenge / confirm / release. Muzeek, Overture, Gigwell, Stagent, SystemOne all do. This is the capability gap that forces agents off generic tools once they pass 3-4 artists. [Hire More Musicians on holds](https://hiremoremusicians.com/what-does-it-mean-to-get-a-hold-in-live-music/).

**What has been abandoned, from public posts and agent commentary:**
- **Salesforce** — too heavy, industry vocabulary wrong, eye-watering pricing.
- **Trello** — too flat, can't represent date-range holds + ranking.
- **Asana** — not gig-aware; tasks ≠ offers.
- **HubSpot** — pipeline metaphor close-ish but doesn't handle holds or split fees.

**Why purpose-built tools also fall short for 1-5 person European shops:**
- Pricing designed for US / UK agencies at larger scale; €39-99/month is livable, €799/month is not.
- Most are anglophone-only interfaces (OK for agents, uncomfortable for artists or Spanish-speaking external admin).
- VAT handling is US-centric; reverse-charge EU workflows require workarounds.
- Multi-role (agent + manager + TM) workflows inside one workspace are often an edge case — the tools assume a clean agent vs. artist separation.

## 8. Collaboration patterns

**Per-artist lead agent.** Inside a 2-5 person agency, every artist has a primary agent — even if the whole roster database is shared. Hand-off between agents is painful because a lot of the value sits in the relationship memory of one person. [Romano Law on key-man provisions](https://www.romanolaw.com/key-contract-terms-for-musicians-and-managers/); [Axis Legal Counsel](https://axislc.com/public/top-legal-issues-and-deal-making-tips-for-negotiating-an-artist-management-agreement-for-musicians-singers-songwriters-music-bands-groups-and-recording-artists/).

**When an artist leaves.** Contractual sunset clauses (typical 3-5 years, tapering) keep the agency entitled to commission on deals initiated before departure. Physical file handover varies wildly: some agencies transfer contact books and ongoing promoter threads cleanly; others wall off contact information as proprietary and force the artist to rebuild. [SmartistU on artist management contracts](https://smartistu.com/artist-management-contracts).

**Manager vs booking agent with shared artist.** Common configuration: an artist has an external manager (handling career / label / press / life) and an agency booker (handling live only). The two roles need different info access:
- Manager sees everything except maybe internal agency fee negotiations.
- Booker sees live only; label / publishing / sync deals are out of scope.
- The **artist themselves** expects a unified view.

Splitting permissions per record (this gig visible to the manager, this contract visible to booker only) is something most generic tools don't model.

**International co-representation.** For artists touring outside the agent's territory, bilateral deals with agents in other territories are standard. Example: a Barcelona-based agent representing a Catalan jazz trio signs a co-booking deal with a US agent for the trio's US tour. The foreign agent pitches, holds, and confirms in-territory; commission is split between the agents on a pre-agreed ratio (60/40 home-agent/foreign is common but it's a full negotiation).

Neither tool inventories this as a first-class concept. Both agencies tend to run their own CRMs in parallel with weekly check-in emails — so the data duplicates, diverges, and occasionally conflicts. Hometown Talent and MN2S are examples of agencies with explicit cross-border touring infrastructure, but the tooling layer is still bespoke. [Hometown Talent via Music Nomad](https://musicnomad.com/Tour/Get-a-Gig/Europe-Booking-Agencies/); [MN2S](https://mn2s.com/).

**Note on mánager vs agent.** In Spanish-language music press "mánager" is used loosely for the career-wide role; "agente" or "booker" specifically for the live-only role. But in day-to-day conversation (and in many Spanish indie acts' own bios) "mánager" is applied to both. Industry associations (IMMF, MMF UK) enforce the distinction. In French, "agent" and "manager" are both used but "agent" leans toward the live role and "manager" toward the career role — same drift as Spanish. [IMMF about us](http://immf.com/about-us/); [MMF UK Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_Managers_Forum).

## 10. Friction stories

The following are reconstructed from forum threads, software-review commentary and interviewee accounts cited above. They are **composite, not direct quotes**, and where a single source is the basis I flag it.

**"Hold expired."** An agent puts a 1st hold on a July festival slot and moves on to other work. Fourteen days pass without a confirmation from the festival; the hold is dropped. Two weeks later, the agent asks to confirm. The festival has since granted the 2nd-hold artist the slot. The roster artist loses the date. Nothing in the agent's CRM reminded them on day 10 that a confirmation was due. [Hire More Musicians hold article](https://hiremoremusicians.com/what-does-it-mean-to-get-a-hold-in-live-music/).

**"The calendar that nobody saw."** An agent receives a new offer and puts the hold in their personal Google Calendar "to check tomorrow" rather than in the roster Airtable. The other agent in the shop, pitching a different promoter for an overlapping weekend, does not see it and double-books the artist. [Generic; representative of commentary on Airtable / Google Drive stacks.]

**"Old rider sent."** The drummer in roster-artist-X got a new backline 8 weeks ago. The rider was updated in the shared Drive folder, but the folder has three rider files from three cycles and the naming is inconsistent. The agent opens the wrong one when advancing a festival, sends the obsolete rider, and the festival PA crew spends an hour reconfiguring on show day. [Off Trail Studios on hosting latest rider via single link](https://www.offtrailstudios.com/post/guide-to-tech-riders-and-stage-plots-for-shows-and-tours).

**"Co-booker conflict."** A Barcelona booker handles Iberia; a German co-booker handles DACH for the same artist. The German co-booker pitches a November tour cluster to DACH venues; the Spanish booker, running the album release campaign, had the album drop scheduled for late November and wanted dates to cluster after launch, not before. Neither tool talks to the other; the misalignment surfaces only when the artist reads both weekly updates and notices.

**"Commission bypass."** A festival settles fees directly to the artist's bank account (common because the artist is the legal supplier for withholding purposes under local artist-tax regimes). The booking agent only finds out because the artist mentions the payment in a casual message. The commission invoice has to be retroactively issued to the artist, and the agent carries the cash-flow hit.

**"Key-man departure."** The agency's lead agent for an artist leaves to start their own shop. The artist, per the management contract's key-man clause, chooses to follow. The agency loses the roster slot, but sunset commissions continue on gigs already contracted. The handover of Drive folders, Gmail labels, contact annotations and tour-in-progress threads is messy and partial. [Romano Law key-man provisions](https://www.romanolaw.com/key-contract-terms-for-musicians-and-managers/).

## 11. What they do NOT want software to do

This is the recurring caveat across every industry interview and forum thread:

- **Replace the relationship.** Every pitch is personal; it is written to the human promoter who remembers last year's tour, not to a procurement portal.
- **Dashboard the artist.** Roster-wide "AI suggestions" and "artist performance scores" are actively rejected by managers interviewed; the decisions are qualitative. [MMF / Music Week context on deal diversity](https://www.musicweek.com/management/read/as-deal-structures-evolve-mmf-publishes-guide-for-music-managers-on-new-commercial-models/090821).
- **Auto-pitch.** Mass-emailing promoters from templated lists is the opposite of the craft. Agents pride themselves on specificity.
- **Algorithmic gig matching.** "This festival is a 74% match" is corporate. Booking is judgment: "is this festival going to be good for the artist's reputation in 3 years" is a question only a human can answer.
- **Feel corporate.** Small European agencies think of themselves as craft shops. UI that looks like Salesforce drives them back to Airtable and Gmail.

What they DO want from software:
- CRM-level admin: contacts, holds, offers, contracts, invoices, payments — tracked without manual bookkeeping.
- Version control on artist materials (latest rider, latest stage plot, latest EPK).
- Calendar that understands holds, not just "events".
- Roster-level overview (every artist's current tour status) without a dashboard that feels invasive.
- Multi-role permission (manager/booker/TM/artist each see what they need).
- VAT-correct invoicing for EU cross-border (reverse charge note, correct NIF / VAT ID fields).

## How this profile differs from Profile 06 (freelance cultural distribution)

For Hour product decisions, this is the load-bearing comparison.

| Axis | Profile 06 freelance distributor | Profile 08 manager / booking agent |
|---|---|---|
| Primary object | The show (as product) | The artist / act (as career) |
| Campaign shape | Per-show, ~12-18 month sales window | Per-album-cycle, layered: live + release + press |
| Inbound rate | Low; mostly outbound cold | Medium-to-high once roster is known |
| Production involvement | Very low; hands off to company | Medium for small artists (agent = TM); low for scaled artists |
| Holds system | Not used; theaters contract directly | Central; 1st/2nd/3rd hold is daily vocabulary |
| Money flows | Commission on closed sale; long invoice tail | Commission per gig + sometimes royalty share; split invoicing; VAT reverse-charge edge cases |
| Artist-life involvement | None (company runs itself) | High, up to flights / visas / personal |
| Core tools | Airtable / Notion / Google / HelloAsso | Airtable + Muzeek / Overture / Stagent / Gigwell |
| Roster size | 3-8 companies | 8-25 artists (booker); 3-8 (manager) |
| Geography of work | Routing per show across EU | Routing per artist across EU + transatlantic co-rep |

**Implication for Hour.** If the thesis "the primary object is the show" holds for Profile 06, it **breaks** for Profile 08 without adaptation. A reasonable compromise: model the primary object as a polymorphic "campaign" that can be a show (theatre) or an artist-cycle (music), and model individual dates (gigs, theatre performances) as children. Holds are a music-specific sub-type of the date entity. This single decision determines whether Hour serves both profiles or forks them.
