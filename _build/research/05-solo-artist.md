# Profile 05 — Solo independent artist

The "one artist, one brand, self-managing" profile. Three sub-flavours share the same operational skeleton but sit in different circuits, with different lead times, different buyers, and different vocabularies:

- **SM — Solo musician / songwriter.** Singer-songwriter, folk, cantautor/a, indie acoustic. Typically 30-50 shows/year when the album cycle is active, fewer in off-years. Buyers are club promoters, festival talent buyers, city councils, salas.
- **SP — Solo performer.** Stand-up, monologuista, spoken word, one-person theatre (teatro de sala, cafe-teatro, comedy club). Tour months skew autumn-winter in the theatre circuit.
- **IC — Independent choreographer.** Self-produces a solo or small-ensemble piece, tours Europe. Lead times 12-18 months. Relies heavily on dance-network infrastructure (Aerowaves, Big Pulse, national dance houses).

Where the three diverge, the section is split SM / SP / IC. Where they converge (which is most of the operational pain), it is left unified.

---

## 1. What they juggle

The defining pressure: **one person is the entire company**. There is no booking agent, no production manager, no label, no A&R, no PR — or if there is, it is a freelancer hired for a discrete job, not a permanent back office.

Typical inventory at any point:

- **SM.** 1 album in current promotion cycle. 1 "dormant" album still being programmed occasionally (festivals that missed the last release, tribute dates, small theatres). 1 side project (acoustic / duo / band lineup / guest appearances). Contact book 200-600 industry names accumulated over 5-10 years: salas, booking programmers, festival bookers, managers of other artists, journalists, radio (some still relevant, most drifting), a PR freelancer or two, a photographer, a merch supplier.
- **SP.** 1 current monologue (the one being toured). 1 previous monologue still available ("if you can't programme the new one we can do X"). Sometimes a "show corto" version (25 min) for galas, company events, festivals. Contact book weighted toward theatre programmers, comedy clubs, empresas privadas (corporate gigs), city councils.
- **IC.** This is where "juggling" is strongest. A touring choreographer typically carries:
  - 1 piece actively touring (the one programmers book).
  - 1 piece in creation (next premiere, 18-30 months out).
  - 1 older piece still in repertoire, re-activated when a theatre asks.
  - Occasional commissions (a short piece for a company, a group-class residency, a pedagogical workshop).
  Contact book is denser than SM/SP because dance touring is slower and more international: 300-800 contacts for a mid-career choreographer, a large share outside Spain.

Across all three, "dormant" works are kept alive in promo because a single email from a programmer two years late can still produce a date. Nothing is truly archived; everything is semi-live.

The *brand* dimension is the other pressure. The artist's name is the product. Website, Instagram, Spotify artist page (SM), press kit, bio — all maintained by the same person who also writes the music / writes the show / choreographs. The creative output and the commercial package are produced by one body. This is why "time to keep the CRM updated" routinely loses to "time to finish the piece".

## 2. Annual cycle

### SM — Solo musician

Cycle is organised around the **album**: studio → release → promo → tour → live → slow fade → new album.

- **Release window:** aim at spring (March-May) or autumn (September-October) to hit festival season and club tours. Summer is festival season. Winter is small venues and theatres. Singer-songwriters often tour theatres in Jan-March (sala-type venue theatre circuit, cultural programming of ayuntamientos).
- **Festival circuit:** Spanish programming decisions for summer festivals (Primavera Sound, Sónar, BBK Live, smaller mid-size) typically close Nov-Feb for the following summer. Small local festivals and fiestas patronales decide Feb-May. Touring outside Spain depends on release and press. (General sector pattern; specific dates vary by festival.)
- **Grant deadlines relevant to SM:** INAEM ayudas a la música (March 2025 convocatoria closed 26 March for the 2025 edition, 8M€ total across danza/lírica/música) [[INAEM 2025](https://www.cultura.gob.es/en/cultura/artesescenicas/contenedora-noticias-prensa/a2025/marzo/ayudas-musica-danza-lirica.html)]. Institut Ramon Llull movilidad has two yearly calls, typically Feb-March for Jan-June activity and July-September for Jul-Dec activity [[IRL 2025](https://www.llull.cat/espanyol/subvencions/mobilitat_musica_esceniques.cfm)].

### SP — Solo performer

Theatre circuit cycle, not album cycle. Tour heavy in **autumn-winter** (Oct-April). Summer is open-air festivals and very targeted gigs (fiestas, festivals de teatro de calle for some, corporate events). Stand-up comedians in Spain also work the club circuit year-round (Madrid Comedy Lab, Tarantos Comedy in Barcelona) [[Tarantos](https://tarantosbarcelona.com/en/tarantos-comedy/)], which runs on a monthly booking logic, not an annual one. Public-theatre programmers tend to close their seasons in May-June for the following September-June season — so a monologuist pitching a 2026-27 theatre tour is writing in spring 2026.

### IC — Independent choreographer

The slowest cycle of the three. A new piece is a **2-4 year arc**:

1. **Creation / research** (6-18 months): residencies, lab periods, sometimes grant-funded research phases.
2. **Residencies** (cumulative): artistic residencies in Spain typically run 1-3 months (Centro Coreográfico Canal Madrid: 4 weeks minimum, 2 months maximum; Matadero Madrid, La Regenta, and others have comparable windows) [[Centro Coreográfico Canal](https://www.recursosculturales.com/residencias-artisticas-centro-coreografico-canal/)][[Matadero](https://www.mataderomadrid.org/en/programs/centre-artists-residence)]. Residencies are often stacked in series across multiple host venues.
3. **Premiere / estreno** at a co-producing theatre or festival.
4. **Tour** (12-30 months post-premiere): this is where bookings close 12-18 months in advance.
5. **Slow fade** / repertoire re-activations.

**Critical deadlines for IC:**
- Aerowaves Twenty annual open call, deadline typically end of August each year (for selection announced in November/December, with performance opportunities the following year) [[Aerowaves](https://aerowaves.org/artists/how-to-apply/)].
- INAEM ayudas a la danza, March each year. 2025 convocatoria included programmes for *giras por España y por el extranjero*, *apoyo a coproducciones interautonómicas*, *programas de apoyo a residencias artísticas de danza* [[INAEM 2025](https://www.cultura.gob.es/en/cultura/artesescenicas/contenedora-noticias-prensa/a2025/marzo/ayudas-musica-danza-lirica.html)].
- Institut Ramon Llull movilidad música y escénicas, two calls per year, EUR 1,000-20,000 per action, 10,000 cap for actions only in Spain [[IRL 2025](https://www.llull.cat/espanyol/subvencions/mobilitat_musica_esceniques.cfm)].
- Culture Moves Europe individual mobility: rolling monthly deadlines across 2025-2026 (Oct 2025 through April 2026 in the current round), groups up to 5 people, performing arts included [[Culture Moves Europe](https://culture.ec.europa.eu/culture-moves-europe)][[On The Move CME](https://on-the-move.org/news/culture-moves-europe-call-individual-mobility-2025-2026)].
- Dance-specific showcases where a piece is visible to programmers: Dansa Metropolitana (March, Barcelona, 9th edition in 2026) [[Dansa Metropolitana 2026](https://www.dansametropolitana.cat/en)], Sismògraf (Olot), Tanzmesse (Düsseldorf, every two years), CINARS (Montreal, biennial).

## 3. Difusión / booking

Alone, with an occasional freelance distributor on retainer for a tour, or splitting the book with an agent who handles only Europe while the artist keeps Spain — all three patterns exist.

### Baseline operation

- Cold email + warm intro + fair attendance. Email is still the primary channel for booking conversations across all three sub-profiles.
- **Sales cycle stages** (approximate, informal naming, observed in practice):
  1. *Primer contacto* — initial pitch, usually cold or warm-intro email, with a link to dossier/video.
  2. *Interés expresado* — programmer says "interesting, send me availabilities" or "when are you touring?"
  3. *Hold* — the programmer pencils the artist on a date pending line-up decision.
  4. *Confirmed* — contract or email confirmation of fee + date + tech.
  5. *Contract signed + dates locked + tech advance complete*.
  6. *Payment received* (often 30-90 days post-show).
- **Ratios (from sector folklore, anecdotal):** a generic cold-email campaign converts 1-5% into a response, and a much smaller fraction into a date. A warm-intro campaign (a programmer you met at a fair, or another artist put you in touch) converts much higher — practitioners cite 20-40%, but this varies. [No robust public data on European touring email conversion for any of the three sub-profiles; treat as ballpark.]

### SM-specific

- Digital-first promo: SubmitHub and Groover are the de facto music pitching tools. Groover guarantees feedback within 7 days at ~2 EUR per submission; SubmitHub is cheaper (free tier + $1-3 premium credits) but shorter feedback [[MusicPulse comparison](https://www.musicpulse.app/blog/submithub-groover-playlistpush-which-service-should-you-choose-in-2026)][[PlaylistHub review](https://playlisthub.io/blog/groover-vs-submithub-which-platform-offers-the-best-music-promotion-for-artists-in-2025/)]. These are playlist/press tools, not festival-booking tools.
- Festival showcase logic: FIMPRO (Guadalajara), Trans Musicales, Mercat de Música Viva de Vic, BIME Pro (Bilbao), Primavera Pro (Barcelona) — artist-showcase fairs that aim to convert one showcase set into multiple bookings.
- Ari Herstand's advice on cold booking emails ("under eight sentences, polite persistence, follow up at least 3 times") circulates widely among anglophone self-managed musicians and has been picked up by Spanish-language cantautor blogs [[Ari's Take cold booking](https://bandzoogle.com/blog/how-to-write-the-perfect-cold-booking-email)][[Ari's Take general](https://aristake.com/)].

### SP-specific

- Theatre circuit: pitching the *programación municipal* (ayuntamientos), circuits like Circuit de la Xarxa (Catalonia), Programa.cat, Red Española de Teatros. A monologuist's bookings concentrate in ayuntamiento-funded theatres and private comedy clubs. Empresas (corporate gigs) are a side channel — often higher fees, zero prestige.
- Stand-up-specific: a weekly open-mic and monthly-showcase rhythm in Madrid and Barcelona builds the material and the reputation. Pitching a full one-hour show to a theatre happens after the material is tested on the club circuit.

### IC-specific — different logic

The dance circuit is programmer-driven and video-driven. Programmers watch integral-video submissions before inviting to visit or programme:

- **Aerowaves Twenty open call**: 15-40 minute finished work, European residency, Vimeo/YouTube link, 100-word description + bio, review at Partner meeting end of October, selection November [[Aerowaves How to Apply](https://aerowaves.org/artists/how-to-apply/)]. The network has ~100 guaranteed performance opportunities per year across partners, both for selected Twenty artists and other applicants [[same]].
- **Big Pulse Dance Alliance** (2021-2024, 12 European dance festivals led by Tanz im August / HAU Berlin, including Sismògraf Catalonia): Visiting Artist Programme, open-air commissions, support for transition from small/medium to larger stages [[Big Pulse](https://www.bigpulsedance.eu/big-pulse-partners/)][[BPDA Visiting Artist](https://on-the-move.org/news/big-pulse-dance-alliance-visiting-artist-programme-sweden-based-choreographers-italy)].
- **Dansa Metropolitana** Barcelona is both a festival and a programmer-visibility moment — 177 performances across 90 shows in the 2026 edition, 15 foreign companies, dance houses across 12 municipalities [[Dansa Metropolitana 2026](https://www.dansametropolitana.cat/en)]. A choreographer showing in Dansa Metropolitana is visible to Catalan, Spanish, and European programmers in one week.
- **Mercat de les Flors**: Casa de la Dansa, a gatekeeper for contemporary dance in Catalonia. Their "El Mercat no m'ha coproduït encara" cycle is an explicit pipeline for emerging artists not yet in the Mercat's co-production chain [[Mercat de les Flors](https://mercatflors.cat/en/)].
- **Tanzmesse** Düsseldorf (biennial, next edition autumn): the single largest dance-sector pitching event. Short showings (20 min) + a curated programme; programmers from 40+ countries in the same 4 days.

The distinction between **pitching a new piece vs. touring a known one** is sharper for IC than for SM/SP:
- *New piece*: dossier, creation-process documentation, excerpts/teaser (because the piece does not exist yet), co-production search. 18-30 month lead.
- *Known piece*: full integral video, press clippings, past venues list, reviews. Often 6-12 month lead. The pitch is shorter because the piece sells itself.

## 4. Production

"Solo" means the artist carries the full operational chain of a company. Advance técnico, load-in, rider negotiation, per diem calculation, travel booking, accommodation, invoicing — all one person.

### Shared across all three

- **Per diems**: EU practice is 25-45 EUR/day depending on country; often negotiated as "dieta" in the contract. [No single authoritative European standard; varies per venue, per contract.]
- **Flights + accommodation**: venues in the dance circuit often include 1-2 nights hotel + 1 flight in the fee; in the music circuit this varies wildly. For solo artists, a venue covering "1 traveller" is fine; for duos/trios it becomes a negotiation.
- **Technical rider**: the solo artist IS the technical contact. They write the rider, they email it to the venue, they re-send it a week before because "our technician has questions". A technical rider for a touring show should be clear and forthright, detail everything the artist brings and everything the venue must supply, and is the product of a negotiation with each venue to match local resources to the rider [[Artist Producer Resource](https://www.artistproducerresource.com/production-sections/technical-rider)][[IMG Stageline](https://www.img-stageline.com/magazine/technical-rider)].

### SM-specific

- Own backline or rental. Singer-songwriter with acoustic guitar travels with the instrument; band-format solo musician may rent backline at destination or require venue-supplied amp. Rider typically: 1 microphone per voice, 1 DI per instrument, 1 monitor, FOH engineer "familiar with acoustic sources".
- Small acoustic shows in theatres sometimes demand a full-band tech ride but only bring the artist + looper + pedalboard. Mismatch is common and avoidable with better advance.

### SP-specific

- Minimal tech. Monologuist often tours with nothing beyond a water bottle and a music cue on a USB. Rider: 1 wireless microphone, 1 technician familiar with the basic light cues, a printed setlist/cue sheet for the operator, a dressing room. Stand-up: no cues, just a mic and a spotlight. This minimal footprint is why monologuists can tour 3-4 shows in a single week.

### IC-specific

- The production complexity is highest. A dance piece needs:
  - Sprung floor or linoleum (dance floor) — venue-supplied but must be confirmed [[Dance Consortium — Production Manager](https://danceconsortium.com/resources/in-and-around-a-dance-company/production-manager/)].
  - Specific dimensions minimum (often 10x10 m or 8x8 m for a solo).
  - Lighting plan: the choreographer carries a plan but counts on venue's par/profile inventory; retuning on-site is common.
  - Sound: often stereo PA with a specific sound operator cue sequence.
  - 1-3 day set-up window including a technical rehearsal and a dress rehearsal.
- Many IC work with a **freelance production manager** hired per tour or per block of dates, and rely on the host venue's **production manager** to interface locally. The Dance Consortium describes exactly this pattern for touring choreographers [[Dance Consortium](https://danceconsortium.com/resources/in-and-around-a-dance-company/production-manager/)].
- Residency co-producers sometimes take on the early-tour production management as part of the co-production deal.

The artist doing their own advance técnico is the default across all three, and it is one of the most error-prone parts of the workflow because the tech rider lives as a PDF that gets updated and re-saved under slightly different filenames (see section 10, friction stories).

## 5. Archive and materials

Per piece / per album, the standard package is:

- Dossier (PDF) — bio, synopsis, credits, tour-dates history, press clippings, photo selection.
- **Video**: full integral (IC, SP), studio recording + live video (SM). For IC, the integral video is the single most-watched asset by programmers before inviting [widely-reported sector practice, referenced in Aerowaves application flow which explicitly requires a Vimeo/YouTube link with password].
- **Teaser / trailer** (1-3 min).
- **Photos**: high-res press photos (3-10 shots, horizontal + vertical, venues want different crops).
- **Technical rider**.
- **Hospitality rider** — dressing room, food, water, vegan option, accessibility (often present, often ignored by smaller venues).
- **Bios** in multiple lengths (100 / 300 / 600 words) and multiple languages (ES minimum, CA when Catalan market, EN for tour, FR for French/Belgian market).
- **Press clippings** — quotes from reviews, star ratings, logos of publications.

Everything lives in **Google Drive or Dropbox**, linked from the website and from email signatures. Vimeo is standard for video, with password lists sent to programmers. Password lists drift (one programmer gets a password for 3 pieces, another for 2 different pieces, the artist forgets who has which). **This is a real and repeated pain point.**

Dossier currency: programmers expect the dossier to reflect the current tour. A dossier with 2022 reviews and 2024 tour dates looks sloppy (see section 10). But keeping dossier content current with tour-history updates is a task routinely deferred.

## 6. Money

### Spain — autónomo

Solo artists in Spain almost always work as **autónomos**. The core fiscal facts for 2025:

- **Régimen especial de retenciones IRPF for artistas**: from 2023 reform (Estatuto del Artista), reduced rates apply to artists under specific income conditions.
- **7% IRPF retention** instead of 15% applies to autónomos artistas whose annual artistic-activity income was under 15,000 EUR in the prior year AND artistic income was >75% of total income [[Legal&Arts](https://abogadosculturales.com/blog/los-artistas-autonomos-podran-aplicar-una-reduccion-del-30-en-el-irpf-a-partir-de-este-2025/)][[Declarando](https://declarando.es/blog/estatuto-artista)].
- From 2025, **30% IRPF reduction on exceptional income** — where income exceeds 130% of the three-prior-years average, the excess can be reduced by 30% [[Autónomos y Emprendedor](https://www.autonomosyemprendedor.es/articulo/hacienda/artistas-autonomos-podran-aplicar-nueva-reduccion-irpf-proximo-ano/20241125173411039571.html)]. This is the "album release year" / "big tour year" relief.
- Relación laboral especial: 2% retention for some specific artist-employment relations (when the artist is under a labour contract to perform, e.g. a theatre season contract) [[Noticias Jurídicas](https://noticias.juridicas.com/actualidad/noticias/17725-los-artistas-autonomos-y-con-relacion-laboral-especial-podran-reducir-su-retencion-irpf-desde-este-jueves/)].

### IVA intracomunitario + OSS

- For services between autónomo in Spain and a company in another EU country, the standard intracomunitario mechanism applies (reverse charge, both parties VAT-registered in their own country's VIES). Invoices leave without VAT; the foreign buyer self-assesses.
- **OSS (ventanilla única)** is relevant primarily for B2C digital services; the 10,000 EUR EU-wide threshold for B2C sales applies [[AEAT OSS](https://sede.agenciatributaria.gob.es/Sede/iva/iva-comercio-electronico/cuestiones-generales.html)][[Your Europe OSS](https://europa.eu/youreurope/business/taxation/vat/one-stop-shop/index_es.htm)]. For most solo performing artists whose buyers are public theatres or promoters (B2B), OSS is not the primary regime. It can come up for merch sales from the artist's website to EU consumers (below threshold → Spanish IVA; above → OSS registration).

### France — intermittent du spectacle

If a solo artist tours France frequently and earns via French employer contracts (CDD through a bureau de production or a festival that hires them short-term), the **intermittent du spectacle** scheme applies:

- **507 hours minimum over 12 months**, across Annex 10 (artists) and/or Annex 8 (technicians) contracts [[Culturepay — devenir intermittent](https://blog.culturepay.fr/intermittent-du-spectacle/devenir-intermittent/)].
- A **cachet = 12 hours** regardless of actual show length [[same]].
- Registration with France Travail (ex-Pôle Emploi) via francetravail.fr/spectacle.
- Unemployment benefit covers the gaps between contracts.

This is not available to a Spanish-resident artist invoicing as autónomo. It only applies when the artist is actually hired in France under French contracts.

### Germany — KSK (Künstlersozialkasse)

For an artist resident in Germany:
- Income floor: **3,900 EUR/year profit** from artistic activity, with a 3-year grace period for new artists [[All About Berlin KSK](https://allaboutberlin.com/guides/ksk-kuenstlersozialkasse)][[bbk Berlin KSK](https://www.bbk-berlin.de/en/faqs-for-artists/kunstlersozialkasse-ksk)].
- Must work in Germany ≥25% of the time.
- KSK covers ~50% of health / pension / long-term care contributions — the artist pays the employee share.
- Cannot have >1 employee.

Again, only relevant for artists actually resident in Germany. A Spanish autónomo working a 3-date German tour does not touch KSK; they invoice the German promoter and handle retention-of-source and IVA intracomunitario.

### Payment delays

Payment delays are **worse for solo artists** because chasing an unpaid invoice is a personal act. Public theatres routinely pay 60-120 days post-show; festivals sometimes worse. The artist is also the person who has to send the reminder, re-send the invoice, call the gestor of the theatre, etc. "I hate chasing money" is a near-universal solo-artist complaint.

### Public grants and accounting

When an artist wins an INAEM / IRL / Culture Moves grant, they take on a parallel accounting obligation: justificación of each euro, deadlines for interim and final reports, receipts for everything. Grant justificación is a job in itself. Most self-managing artists use a gestoría for tax and a separate internal system (Excel, Notion table) for grant tracking.

## 7. Tools today and why they fail

The **baseline stack** for a solo artist is consistent across the three sub-profiles:

- **Gmail** — primary communication. Everything lives here. Labels drift. Search is the only navigation.
- **Google Calendar** — dates, holds, travel.
- **Google Drive / Dropbox** — dossiers, riders, photos, videos master files.
- **Vimeo** — integral videos and teasers. Password-protected links for programmer reviews.
- **Apple Notes** — half of the artist's brain lives here. Lyrics, creative notes, to-do lists, pitch drafts.
- **WhatsApp** — distributor / production manager / venue technical contacts. Threads get buried.
- **Instagram + Spotify for Artists (SM)** — public brand.

Beyond baseline, common attempts:

- **Notion heavily** — especially among the "personal OS" artists. Templates like the Musician & Artist Workspace exist [[Notion Musician template](https://www.notion.com/templates/musician)][[Notioneer Artist Workspace](https://notioneer.gumroad.com/l/artist)][[Landmark Notion for Musicians guide](https://www.landmarklabs.co/blog/notion-for-musicians-ultimate-guide-2024)]. A specific, documented pattern: the artist tries 3-5 different Notion templates from YouTube and abandons them because they need fields the template doesn't have, or the template requires daily maintenance the artist won't do. This is the "I've tried 5 Notion templates and none stuck" pattern [anecdotal, widely cited in self-managing artist forums and Reddit, no hard data].
- **Airtable** — artists who think in tables use it as a CRM (contacts + last-contact + status). Skillshare and the like have specific "Airtable for Artists" classes [[Airtable for Artists](https://www.skillshare.com/en/classes/Airtable-for-Artists-Organize-Your-Business-Increase-Your-Productivity/2144496589)].
- **Asana** — tried, found too heavy for a single-person operation.
- **Trello** — tried, found too flat; the artist outgrows it the minute they need to link contacts to pieces to dates.

### Why tools fail for solo artists

1. **Single-person bottleneck.** Daily input tools fail because the artist is sometimes in a 3-week creation residency with no capacity for admin. Any workflow that demands daily entry breaks.
2. **Specificity gap.** Generic CRMs (HubSpot, Pipedrive) are too heavy and speak the wrong language (sales funnel, not touring cycle). Artist templates on Notion/Airtable get the vocabulary closer but still require heavy customisation.
3. **Object mismatch.** Tools model "contacts + deals" or "projects + tasks". The actual primary object for this profile is **the piece / the show / the album** — with contacts, dates, materials, and money hanging off it. No mass-market tool maps this directly. (This is the thesis of Hour — see project CLAUDE.md.)
4. **Materials drift.** Dossier PDFs, photo packs, and videos live in Drive folders that grow organically, so the artist ends up emailing programmers links to v3.2-final-FINAL.pdf.

## 8. Collaboration patterns

Alone by default, freelance-augmented occasionally. The recurring collaborators:

- **Freelance distributor** (SM / SP — less common for IC, who work more through network channels). Paid a commission on confirmed dates (10-20%). Often works with several artists in parallel.
- **Production manager freelance** (IC mostly; SM for larger tours only). Paid daily rate during the tour.
- **PR / comunicación** (SM album release; IC at premiere moment). Campaign-based fee.
- **Photographer / designer** (dossier, press photos, album art, poster). Project fee.
- **Gestor / gestoría** (fiscal, always). Monthly fee.

### Tension: handing off to a distributor

The "I don't want to lose my relationships" fear is concrete:

- The artist has built the contact book personally over years.
- A distributor coming on board needs access to that history to work effectively.
- But the artist is reluctant to hand over the full contact list, both because of personal relationships and because if the distribution deal ends, the contacts may walk.

In practice this often produces a **split book**: the distributor gets access to "prospects and open conversations" but the artist keeps a private layer of "long friendships / warm intros / sensitive context". Tools that force a single shared contact list are resisted; tools that allow explicit sharing of *some* conversations with *some* people are a better fit.

### The solo artist as primary communicator

Even when a distributor is hired, many programmers want to talk directly to the artist — especially in dance, where the programmer's decision is partly an artistic one and the artist's presence matters. This is different from a band on a tour, where the booker is the operational face. Solo artists rarely achieve full delegation of booking conversations.

### Circuit differences

- **Music circuit** uses *bolo, concierto, sala, cachet, festival, fanbase*. Vocabulary is more Anglicised (indie rock borrowings: *gig, show, set, headline, support*).
- **Dance circuit** uses *peça, funció, programador, residència, estrena, coproducció*. Vocabulary is more European-institutional (more French and German borrowings: *création, tournée, pièce, studio*).
- **Theatre circuit** (SP) overlaps more with dance: *función, programador, sala, gira, caché*. Stand-up specifically imports English terms wholesale: *open mic, showcase, bit, set, punchline, crowd work*.

A product that serves all three must let the artist choose the noun they use for "the primary object" — *piece / show / album / piece / peça* — and the noun for "the date" — *gig / función / performance / bolo / concierto*.

## 10. Friction stories

Anecdotal and composite — representative of patterns reported across multiple sources and conversations. Not attributed to individual artists.

1. **The outdated dossier.** A touring choreographer sends a dossier in March 2024 to book autumn 2024. The dossier's press-clippings page stops in 2022 (last batch of reviews the artist processed). The tour-dates list has been updated through 2024. A programmer in Lille opens it, thinks "is this piece still active?" and doesn't reply. The artist never finds out. Lost date because the dossier wasn't reconciled.

2. **The email in Promotions.** A solo musician, mid-tour, gets a festival booking offer. Gmail's Promotions tab swallows it (the festival sent from a marketing-automation address). The artist discovers it 3 weeks later — the slot is gone. "I don't trust my inbox" becomes a permanent low-grade anxiety.

3. **The gestor and the missed invoice deadline.** A solo artist finishes a Spanish public grant (INAEM justificación). Two invoices sit unpaid — the artist hasn't issued them because the promoter hasn't sent the VAT data. The gestor charges extra for the scramble to close the justificación before the deadline. A coordinated grant-tracking system (as opposed to scattered email threads) would have flagged this three weeks earlier.

4. **The wrong rider on arrival.** A choreographer arrives at a residency for piece B. The venue technician has read the rider for piece A (the artist sent the wrong PDF 4 months ago, the venue never asked again). Day 1 of a 5-day residency is lost reconfiguring lights. Trust in the venue drops. Trust in the artist's professionalism also drops.

5. **The 1/50 cold email campaign.** A monologuist sends 50 near-identical emails to theatre programmers in Spain and Portugal for the 2026-27 season. One reply. Pitches were generic, used the wrong name in one case ("Estimado/a programador/a"), and linked a dossier that needed a password which was at the bottom of the email in 8pt grey text. A better-researched 15-email campaign with personalised first lines would outperform, but the artist wrote the 50 in one 2-hour burst at the end of a Sunday.

6. **The password spreadsheet.** A choreographer maintains a Google Sheet of Vimeo passwords — one password per piece, shared over time with ~40 programmers. The artist decides to rotate a password after a video leaks to a pirated site. Cannot send the new password only to the 40 legitimate recipients because the list is scattered across 3 years of emails. Ends up rotating + emailing a generic "new password" blast to everyone, which signals amateurism.

7. **The residency two-month blackout.** A choreographer enters a 2-month creation residency in October. Incoming booking emails pile up. On re-emerging, 30 unread threads — some old enough that the programmer has already booked someone else. "I was making the work" is a valid explanation but doesn't recover the lost dates. This is the structural tension in section 11: creative time IS non-admin time, and the software must accept that.

## 11. What they do NOT want software to do

Strong pattern across all three sub-profiles:

- **The creative practice is private.** Scores, sketches, notes, drafts, rehearsal journals, voice memos of song ideas, movement logs — these are **not for the tool**. Artists protect this from employers, managers, partners. They will not put it in a shared workspace, not even a private one, if the workspace has "business" framing. This is psychological, not technical.
- **No daily-input requirement.** A tool that requires a 5-minute daily check-in is a tool that dies during a creation period. "I'll fill it in once every 2-3 months" must be a valid usage pattern. The tool must survive extended neglect and be immediately useful on return.
- **No replacement for email.** Booking conversations happen in email. The tool should *capture* what happened in email (attach emails, log decisions) but not *replace* email. Solo artists will not move to a new messaging layer and ask their programmers to follow them there.
- **No gamification.** Streaks, leaderboards, "you closed 3 deals this week" framing — deeply off-brand for all three sub-profiles, and especially for IC.
- **No AI voice rewrite of their bios.** The bio is a brand artifact. Machine-rewritten bios feel wrong and the artist can tell within two lines.
- **No social-media publishing.** Artists manage their Instagram personally (or, if not, through a trusted collaborator). They do not want a CRM tool that schedules posts.
- **Must handle the public-facing side only.** Dossier logistics, contact tracking, date tracking, material versioning, money tracking — yes. Creative process — no.

## Gaps and caveats

- **Conversion ratios** (cold email → date) in section 3 are practitioner folklore. No public dataset exists for European touring-artist email conversion. Treat as ballpark.
- **"5 Notion templates, none stuck"** pattern in section 7 is anecdotal. Widely reported in artist forums and Reddit, but no quantitative survey.
- **Per diem ranges** (25-45 EUR/day) in section 4 are sector norm, not a regulated figure.
- **Interview sources** for Vero Cendoya, Sònia Gómez, Juan Dominguez, Zahara, Joan Miquel Oliver were not located as dedicated long-form interviews during this pass. The profiles above are built from their official sites and third-party bios; primary-source interviews with these artists should be added if the research budget allows.
- **Stand-up / monologuista circuit data in Spain** is thin in public sources. The "autumn-winter tour months" claim is sector norm but not quantified. A direct conversation with a booker at Tarantos Comedy or Madrid Comedy Lab would tighten this.
- **ijet** (independent choreographers reference) — mentioned in the task brief but no strong public site located. May be an informal network; flagged for follow-up.
- **The "I'll fill it in once a year" valid-workflow requirement** (section 11) is a strong anecdotal pattern but has not been validated against a specific user interview in this pass.
