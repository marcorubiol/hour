# Profile 03 — Small music production company / indie label / booking agency

A small Spanish or Catalan music company of 2-5 staff handling a roster of 5-15 artists. The company may be a pure indie label, a pure booking agency, a management-plus-booking hybrid, or a label-plus-booking-plus-management triple-hat operation — in Spain the boundary between these functions is porous in companies at this scale. Reference points used in this document: Foehn Records (Barcelona, indie label, ~20-25 active/archive artists, founded 2001) ([foehnrecords.com](https://www.foehnrecords.com/label/), [discogs](https://www.discogs.com/label/6416-Foehn-Records)); Whisper Not (Barcelona, booking + management, created 2013) ([whispernotagency.com](https://whispernotagency.com/)); Red Mango Music (Barcelona, booking, ex-NBC Productions) ([redmangomusic.es](https://redmangomusic.es/en/about-us/)); Hook Management (Sounds From Spain directory) ([soundsfromspain.com](https://www.soundsfromspain.com/en/companies/hook-management/)); DCS4 Agency (Barcelona underground scene) ([dcs4.ch](https://dcs4.ch)). Public-facing roster pages for Hidden Track and Primavera Agencia did not surface in search — No solid data on their current roster size.

This document serves the Hour design brief: the "show" (here: a live gig, a tour, a release) is the primary object, and everything — contact, date, material, money — hangs off it. In this profile the aggregation is pushed one level further: the artist is a folder of shows, and the roster is a folder of artists.

## 1. What they juggle

A roster of 5-15 artists sits on a staff of 2-5 (often 2-3). Each artist has their own rhythm, which means the office is always handling several contradictory cycles at once:

- one artist is mid-tour;
- a second is in the studio recording and going dark on live dates for 4 months;
- a third has released nothing in 18 months and is technically still on the roster but dormant — the "active vs archive" distinction is blurry and rarely written down;
- a fourth just signed and needs EPK, photos, contract, bio, socials connected, and a Bandcamp page inside 2-3 weeks;
- a fifth is being pitched to festivals for next summer;
- a sixth is in a sync-licensing conversation that will close or die in the next 90 days.

Typical contact book: the working estimate across agencies at this size is hundreds to low thousands of individual programmers, promoters, festivals, press, radio, sync supervisors, booking counterparties, managers, and tour support contacts. Nobody has published a clean baseline for the Spanish scene specifically — anecdotal reports on Reddit and blog interviews put mid-career indie booking agencies at 500-2,000 addressable contacts, but this is not peer-reviewed data.

Parallel campaigns are the norm. Within any 60-day window the company is simultaneously running: one or two album release campaigns, tour routing for 3-5 artists in different touring windows, festival application batches (see §2), a couple of sync pitches, press cycles for each release, and ongoing low-level difusión ("promoter X said call back in May"). The product implication: the unit of work cannot be "a project" — it must be a show/release/campaign with per-artist roll-up, because a tour and a release for the same artist are distinct work streams that must converge at a launch.

## 2. Annual cycle

Two clocks run in parallel: the artist-by-artist clock and the office-wide clock.

**Office-wide fixed calendar** is anchored on European showcase festivals, which are the mandatory trade fairs where a booking agent gets 40-80% of the next year's foreign dates:

- **Eurosonic Noorderslag (ESNS)** — Groningen, mid-January. The 2026 edition is 14-17 January; applications for 2027 open 1 May 2026 ([esns.nl via musicexport.at](https://www.musicexport.at/service-information/showcasing/)). 4,000+ delegates, 400+ international festivals attending.
- **Jazzahead!** — Bremen, April. 2026 dates: 22-25 April ([jazzahead.de via musicexport.at](https://www.musicexport.at/service-information/showcasing/)).
- **Primavera Pro** — Barcelona, early June, parallel to Primavera Sound. 2026 dates: 3-7 June. Meeting point where "the state of the music business is debated with experts and dozens of newcomer bands showcase" ([primaverasound.com](https://www.primaverasound.com/en/barcelona)).
- **Reeperbahn Festival** — Hamburg, September. Describes itself as "one of the most important meeting places for the music industry worldwide" and Europe's largest club festival.
- **SXSW** — Austin, March. Outside Europe but still a calendar anchor for agencies with a North America angle.

Application season for most of these runs roughly September-January, with application windows published on each festival's site. Spanish-circuit festivals (Primavera Sound itself, Sónar, BIME, Mad Cool, FIB, Vida, Cruïlla, Canela Party, Tomavistas, among others) typically confirm lineups in waves between December and April for summer dates — the booking conversation often opens in September-October of the previous year.

**Per-artist clock — album release.** A full album release campaign runs 6-9 months of pre-release work. The consensus from release-plan literature ([Cyber PR Music](https://cyberprmusic.com/music-release-plan/), [DIY Musician / CD Baby](https://diymusician.cdbaby.com/releasing-music/music-release-strategy-2025/), [Orphiq](https://orphiq.com/resources/how-far-in-advance-plan-music-release)): 12-16 weeks lead for an EP or album, 6+ months for an album with multi-single rollouts, 6 weeks minimum for distribution to DSPs via TuneCore / CD Baby / DistroKid, and 3-4 months of lead time if a publicist is targeting major press. Vinyl lead times (not in those sources but widely reported in industry trade press) push the physical side to 4-6 months.

**Tour windows.** Spanish circuit rhythm in practice: April-June (festival season + late-spring club dates), late September-November (autumn club circuit), December dead zone except for special end-of-year shows, January-March slow rebuild around Eurosonic-related international routing. Summer July-August is festival-heavy in Spain but clubs close in many cities.

The product implication: a single calendar view cannot be enough. The office needs (a) a per-artist timeline of release + tour, (b) an office-wide calendar of all artist dates overlaid, (c) a rolling "festival-application season" list keyed to showcase deadlines.

## 3. Difusión / booking

Booking is the core revenue motion for the booking-agency-shaped part of this profile. The mechanics:

**Offer flow.** Offer → counter-offer → confirm → contract → advance (details) → settlement. The offer carries fee + date + technical fit; the counter-offer usually moves fee, adds a day-off radius clause (no competing show within X km / Y days), or adjusts hospitality. Confirm triggers the contract draft. Contract signed triggers advance. ([AIP Promoter Jargon Buster](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5baea4c47fdcb8014a0545c3/t/611b786242fb8171030d83ee/1629190243116/AIP+Promoter+Jargon+Buster.pdf) for glossary.)

**Ratios.** The "1 in 10" figure for cold pitches is folk wisdom, not documented — Jazzfuel and industry blogs quote numbers ranging from 0.5% (1 in 200 cold emails) to ~10% on the optimistic end, with the note that "even 10% can be a stretch if you are doing a lot of this" ([jazzfuel.com](https://jazzfuel.com/cold-emailing-for-gigs/)). Inbound ratios from known promoters (existing relationships) are dramatically higher — anecdotal reports put them at 40-70% conversion when a promoter is inbound asking about an artist. No solid public data on Spanish-specific conversion rates.

**Roster management — which artist to push when.** This is a deeply human skill and is where the small agency's advantage lives. A new signing with a fresh record and buzz is "sold forward" — pushed to every booker, every festival, every playlist. A mid-career artist with solid but undramatic numbers is "placed" — matched to specific known-friendly promoters and routed around existing European dates to amortise travel. A dormant artist is parked. The agency routinely has to tell a promoter "not X, but try Y" — which means the internal system has to make roster-wide recommendations visible, not just one-artist views.

**Gigmit cold-email guide** ([gigmit blog](https://blog.gigmit.com/en/10-cold-email-tips-for-bookings/)) captures the mechanics of how pitches are personalised per promoter — the practical cost for a 15-artist agency is that each booker writes dozens of bespoke emails a week, and nobody has visibility into what went out or what the response was unless it was BCC'd to a team alias.

## 4. Production

Production control at this company size is deliberately minimal. The agency almost never tours with the artist — the artist has their own tour manager, or the tour is DIY and one band member is the de facto TM. The agency holds:

- the current rider and stage plot (and the responsibility to keep them current);
- the advance — i.e., the pre-show document exchange with the promoter covering arrival, load-in, soundcheck, hospitality, catering, accommodation, ground transport, settlement (cash vs transfer), merch cut, payout timing, contact chain. See [tourmanager.info/advancing-shows](https://tourmanager.info/advancing-shows/) and [Ari's Take](https://aristake.com/how-to-properly-advance-your-shows-and-why-you-have-to/) for the canonical checklist.
- the contract + confirmation + deposit status;
- the liaison with the artist's manager or TM.

What's handed off to the TM: day-of-show execution, expense reconciliation, merch desk handling, post-show settlement sheet capture. The agency then receives the settlement photo and the bank transfer confirmation, and files them.

**Festival advance** differs from club advance: typically a festival uses a portal (Muzeek, Festicket's internal tools, a bespoke form) because they're advancing 40-100 artists at once ([festivalpro.com](https://www.festivalpro.com/festival-management/1070/news/2020/10/14/The-Importance-of-the-Artist-Advance-Sheet-for-Events-and-Festivals.html)). The agency then mirrors those details back into their own system — a known duplication point.

**The advance bottleneck.** When 15 artists have shows the same week, advance traffic explodes. Every show needs hospitality confirmed, tech specs updated, ground transport, guest lists. The booker is not scaling — the same human is doing advances across the roster, often while negotiating next-season dates. This is where small agencies break: an advance gets dropped, a rider isn't re-sent, the promoter shows up with a wrong backline, and the band is furious. Prism.fm's marketing material leans on exactly this pain ([prism.fm blog](https://prism.fm/blog/event-management-software/managing-your-rosters-calendar/)), as does Overture ([bookingwithoverture.com](https://bookingwithoverture.com/)), claiming "a missed hold can cost 5-15% of an artist's annual touring income" ([via Stagent](https://stagent.com/blog/how-to-start-a-booking-agency)).

## 5. Archive and materials

Every artist has a folder. The folder contains: current EPK (one-pager), high-res press photos (usually 3-5 per cycle), live photos, low-res web photos, videos (live, studio, promo), current bio (short + long, in ES/CA/EN), stream/DSP links, current rider (tech + hospitality), stage plot, input list, sales sheet for programmers, contract template, artist fiscal data (NIF, IBAN, retention rate).

Multiply by 15 artists, and multiply by 2-4 release cycles over 5 years, and you have:

- the "which folder is the current rider" problem — often 4-6 versions of the same rider file, last-modified dates lying because someone re-uploaded an old one;
- the "bio is stale" problem — the English bio still says "recently released their debut in 2022";
- the "photo rights" problem — a press photo was by a specific photographer, the credit is required, the photographer is no longer reachable, nobody knows if the label still has the licence;
- the "which track is sync-cleared" problem (relevant for labels with sync activity — see §6).

The [2026 Musician EPK Checklist](https://aboutmysound.com/blog-posts/the-2026-musician-epk-checklist-what-booking-agents-actually-want-to-see) lists what booking agents want to see as: bio (short + long), hi-res photos with credit, 2-3 live videos, streaming links, press quotes, tour history, contact. Industry EPK tools (Bandzoogle, ReelCrafter, Sonicbids) exist but are artist-facing — the agency-side problem is aggregating + versioning across 15 artists, not authoring one.

Empirical claim: agencies rebuild their asset library into a new tool every 18-36 months because each tool becomes a mess. Not directly sourced — this is Marco's field observation validated by several Reddit r/MusicBusiness threads (anecdotal) and by Relationl's marketing ([digitalmusicnews.com](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2025/09/15/relationl-music-companies-modernize-systems-airtable/)) which explicitly sells the "Airtable + consulting" rebuild service to music companies.

## 6. Money

Money is the hairiest part of this profile, and the part most routinely done wrong.

**Commission splits.** Booking agents in this segment typically charge 10-15% of gross performance fee to the artist, sometimes 15-20% for a newer artist requiring heavy lifting ([Stagent](https://stagent.com/blog/how-to-start-a-booking-agency), [Josiah Soren](https://www.josiahsoren.com/articles/music-industry-professional-roles-rates-and-responsibilities), [Harmony Central forum](https://www.harmonycentral.com/forums/topic/7143-what-percentage-do-booking-agents-get/)). Managers (when separate) typically take 15-20% of the artist's gross, sometimes up to 25% for new artists ([Grammy.com career guide](https://go.grammy.com/music-careers/music-agent/), [ArtistPR](https://www.artistpr.com/music-promotion/music-manager/what-percentage-of-your-earnings-should-your-music-manager-get/)). When the same small Spanish company is both booking and management, the effective take from the artist can be 25-35% of gross live income.

**Label/booker/manager separation.** For labels: master-recording royalties flow label → artist on a release-by-release schedule, typically 50/50 net after recoupment in indie deals (no single source — this is industry consensus, not a citable standard). Live fees flow through booking. Publishing (SGAE / direct deal) flows separately.

**Who invoices the promoter.** Three patterns exist in Spain:

1. **Agency invoices the full fee, pays the artist net** — agency keeps its commission on their own books. Cleaner for the promoter, messier for the artist (they have to trust the agency's accounting).
2. **Split invoicing** — the promoter receives two invoices, one from the artist for the performance fee and one from the agency for its commission ([Vocalist.org.uk](https://vocalist.org.uk/entertainment-agents), "an agency invoice is an invoice by the agency addressing the promoter … that includes only the agency fee. An invoice that states only the artist performance fee is required separately").
3. **Artist invoices, pays commission back to agency** — cleanest for the artist, slowest for the agency.

All three are used in Spain depending on promoter preference, artist autónomo status, and VAT treatment.

**VAT: intra-EU reverse charge.** When a Spanish agency/artist performs for a non-Spanish EU promoter, the place of supply for live artistic services is normally where the event takes place ([EU VAT rules summary, Avalara](https://www.avalara.com/us/en/vatlive/eu-vat-rules/eu-vat-returns/reverse-charge-on-eu-vat.html), [Marosa VAT](https://marosavat.com/vat-reverse-charge/), [Agencia Tributaria](https://sede.agenciatributaria.gob.es/Sede/en_gb/iva/iva-operaciones-comercio-exterior/prestaciones-servicios.html)). In practice invoices often go out with reverse-charge reference and no Spanish VAT charged; the receiving promoter self-accounts. Mistakes here generate the classic "festival paid 21% less than expected because they withheld VAT" re-invoicing loop.

**Retenciones (IRPF withholding) in Spain.** For Spanish autónomos performing in Spain, invoices to Spanish businesses normally carry an IRPF retention. Rates moved under the Estatuto del Artista reforms: the general autónomo artist rate was reduced — artists with prior-year artistic income under €15,000 (and where that income is >75% of total) may apply a reduced 7% IRPF retention instead of 15%; a separate 2% rate applies under the artistic special-labour-relationship regime ([Ministerio de Cultura release, 2023](https://www.cultura.gob.es/actualidad/2023/01/230124-irpf-estatuto-artista.html), [autonomosyemprendedor.es](https://www.autonomosyemprendedor.es/articulo/hacienda/retencion-irpf-artistas-requisitos/20240428203012035929.html), [TaxDown](https://taxdown.es/blog/reduccion-irpf-artistas)). The agency often has to know each artist's correct retention rate and annotate every invoice. This is not optional — wrong retention = Hacienda problem downstream.

**SGAE.** Performing rights on live shows in Spain are collected by SGAE at the venue/promoter level, generally 8.5% of box office (disputed — Last Tour / BIME argued this rate was "abusive" and the dispute has been in litigation through 2025-2026) ([IQ Magazine, Oct 2025](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/10/spains-sgae-condemns-promoter-over-alleged-non-payment-of-royalties/), [Digital Music News, Oct 2025](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2025/10/31/sgae-last-tour-dispute/)). SGAE distributes performing-right royalties monthly since 2022 ([Creative Industries News, 2022](https://creativeindustriesnews.com/2022/01/spains-sgae-starts-distributing-live-performance-royalties-on-a-monthly-basis/)). The agency doesn't usually collect SGAE on behalf of the artist but may help the artist correctly register setlists so the distribution is accurate. Some Spanish artists have been leaving SGAE — ~200 musicians + 50 publishers registered withdrawal in 2019 ([Digital Music News, 2019](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2019/10/04/sgae-artists-leaving/)) — for direct licensing or alternative PROs.

**The "fee is X but net-to-artist is Y" problem.** A €3,000 festival slot in Germany, for a Spanish agency-represented Spanish artist, might actually deliver (illustrative, not sourced):

- €3,000 gross fee, reverse-charge VAT (no VAT charged to promoter);
- minus agency commission 10-15% → €2,550-2,700 net to artist;
- minus SGAE share (if collected separately — depends);
- minus retention if any;
- minus travel, per diems, accommodation (sometimes buy-out, sometimes hospitality-included);
- minus band-member shares → what the leader actually takes home.

The calculation is done 40-80 times a year per artist. Getting it wrong is how artists leave rosters.

## 7. Tools used today and why they fail

The observed stack across small Spanish and European indie agencies/labels:

- **Gmail** — usually the team inbox. Some use Google Workspace shared inboxes (`info@agency.com`, `bookings@agency.com`) routed to all staff; others use individual inboxes with BCC conventions ("always BCC bookings@ on offer threads"). Shared inbox vs individual inbox is a live debate. The "a promoter emails X artist and nobody knows who's on that account" problem is a direct consequence of bad inbox discipline.
- **Google Drive / Dropbox** — the canonical artist folder store. Folder structure is improvised per agency, with hand-maintained conventions like `/Artists/BandName/2026/Press` and `/Artists/BandName/2026/Tech`. Versioning is manual ("final_v3_FINAL_REAL").
- **Excel / Google Sheets** — the gig tracker. A roster-wide sheet with rows = gigs, columns = artist, date, city, venue, promoter, fee, status (offer/hold/confirm/contract/advanced/played/paid), notes. Often per-artist tabs. Breaks at 10+ artists.
- **Airtable** — the scaled-up gig tracker. Widely adopted. [Digital Music News, Sep 2025](https://www.digitalmusicnews.com/2025/09/15/relationl-music-companies-modernize-systems-airtable/) and [Relationl](https://www.relationl.co/) directly market Airtable consulting to music companies — Warner, RCA, Spirit, Big Machine, Reservoir are quoted as Airtable users. At 5-15 artist scale, the pattern is: an internal person or contractor builds a custom Airtable base (artists, gigs, contacts, offers, contracts), it works for 12-24 months, then it decays and is rebuilt. [Orphiq's write-up](https://orphiq.com/resources/talent-agency-software) notes bluntly: "Airtable is not music-specific, you will spend weeks building custom fields, templates, and views before the tool does anything useful for your roster, with no built-in royalty tracking, release-aware date logic, or DSP integration."
- **Notion** — used for internal docs, wikis, release plans, artist checklists. Rarely the primary CRM because Notion's relational side is weaker than Airtable's.
- **Muzeek** — festival-facing advance tool. Widely used by festivals; agencies interact with it as a guest. Starts around $50/month ([muzeek.com](https://muzeek.com/)).
- **Overture** — agency-side booking tool, ~350 agencies, 55k agents/artists claimed ([bookingwithoverture.com](https://bookingwithoverture.com/)).
- **Prism.fm** — venue + promoter + agent tool, 1,500+ venues/promoters claimed ([prism.fm](https://prism.fm/)). Often experienced from the promoter side — the agency sees Prism invites but may not subscribe itself.
- **ABOSS** — European live-music-specific booking operating system, pitched as Airtable-replacement for artist booking agencies ([abossmusic.com](https://www.abossmusic.com/), [systemonesoftware.com comparison](https://www.systemonesoftware.com/blog/aboss-alternatives)).
- **Gigwell** — booking CRM, heavier feature set, trends toward larger agencies ([gigwell.com](https://www.gigwell.com/blog/gigwell-vs-prism)).
- **Stagent, Backstage Hub, Rockhouse** — adjacent tools, smaller footprint in Spanish market; no solid data on Spanish penetration.

**Abandoned:** Trello and Asana — both cited in forum discussions as tried and dropped because they're project-management-shaped and not roster-shaped. Trello's lists-and-cards model does not survive 15 artists × 40-80 gigs = 600-1,200 cards. Asana's project model does not naturally carry "artist" as a first-class entity.

**Why the Airtable pattern keeps resetting.** Every rebuild introduces a new schema, new view set, new access rules. The data migrates imperfectly, people use the new base for new work but keep reaching back to the old base for anything >1 year old, and within 18 months the new base accumulates the same warts as the old one. This is the "bespoke-SQL-in-Airtable shops" pattern and is Hour's natural target.

## 8. Collaboration patterns

**Office-wide visibility.** At 2-5 staff, everybody needs to see the roster-wide calendar. A booker owns their primary artists but covers for colleagues on holiday or on parental leave. A manager needs to see "which dates are already confirmed" before pitching a new offer. This is the "calendar-with-no-lock" failure mode cited in §10.

**Artist-facing vs internal.** Artists themselves want a cleaner view: "what shows do I have confirmed?", "what's the fee on each?", "where do I fly into?". Agencies handle this by: sending the artist an updated calendar PDF monthly, giving them a stripped-down Airtable view, or (most commonly) not giving them a systematic view and answering per-email. This is an unmet need — the artist wants a portal, not a PDF.

**External collaborators.** Tour managers need per-tour access to advance docs, hotel confirmations, contact sheets. PR and radio pluggers need the EPK, promo links, press quotes. Sync agents need cleared-master metadata. A shared Google Drive folder is the typical answer; access revocation when a relationship ends is inconsistent.

**When an artist leaves the roster.** The messy case. The artist's folder is split between the shared Drive (agency-owned), the booker's individual laptop, a personal Dropbox somebody used for overflow, and email threads across 2-3 staff inboxes. Formal handover to the artist or the new representation is often partial — bios and current photos are handed over, historical offer correspondence usually isn't. No canonical playbook exists; this is a friction point Hour can own.

**Management vs booking vs label — one system or several?** Smaller Spanish companies (hybrids) typically run one system because two would be worse. Larger companies sometimes split: Gigwell for booking, a label-ops tool for release workflow, Google Drive for everything else. Hour's bet, per project brief: one system, with roles and per-role views.

## 10. Friction stories

Anecdotal patterns observed across Reddit r/MusicBusiness, agency blogs, and practitioner interviews. These are canonical failure shapes rather than verified single-company incidents.

1. **Double-booking on shared Excel.** The gig calendar is a Google Sheet. One booker confirms a 14 June Toulouse date. A second booker (covering while the first is on holiday) offers the same artist a 14 June Madrid date that was inbound from a known promoter. Both get confirmed before either refreshes the sheet. One has to be cancelled, one relationship burns. Root cause: no write-lock on the row, no notification on conflict, no "is this artist held this day?" check at offer time.

2. **Unowned account.** A promoter emails `bookings@agency.com` about Artist X. The shared inbox shows 3 unread. Neither of the 3 staff claim the thread. Four days pass. The promoter calls the artist directly and asks what's going on. The artist is furious because "my agency is asleep." Root cause: inboxes are not account-owner aware.

3. **New signing, slow materials.** Artist signs in late January; agency wants to pitch to summer festivals with a deadline of mid-February. The EPK, bio, and hi-res photos take three weeks to land in the shared Drive — late for the second wave of pitches. Root cause: no checklist-per-new-artist, no deadline-driven onboarding flow.

4. **VAT re-invoicing loop.** French festival pays €2,500 into the agency account against a €3,000 invoice. On inspection, the festival's finance department has applied French VAT withholding anyway, despite the reverse-charge note. The agency spends 3 weeks and 2 emails to the festival's fiscalista getting the €500 released, or issues a corrective invoice. Happens 3-5 times per year per agency, per practitioner anecdote.

5. **The leaving-artist folder hemorrhage.** Artist leaves for another agency. On request, the old agency tries to hand over files. 60% of the material is in the shared Drive. 20% is on the booker's laptop. 15% is in a personal Dropbox a previous booker set up. 5% is in closed email threads. The artist's new manager spends 4 weeks rebuilding.

6. **Rider version confusion.** Promoter receives a rider in March for a June show. Between March and June the artist changes monitor engineer, changes backline preference, and updates the input list. The promoter shows up on the day with the March rider. Conflict at load-in.

7. **Advance storm.** One week in October: 6 of the 14 artists have shows Thu-Sun. The agency has one person doing advances. By Wednesday she has done 4, not 6. Two shows go in half-advanced. One had a hotel not booked. Predictable, recurring.

## 11. What they do NOT want software to do

The list is sharp and consistent across practitioner blogs and interviews:

- **Taste-making and curation.** "We believe in this band" is the founding bet of an indie label. No software writes the signing decision.
- **Human filtering of offers.** Deciding whether to take a €400 club show in Zaragoza because the promoter has historically opened doors for touring companies is a judgement call. Software can surface the offer; it must not rank or auto-decline.
- **Negotiation tone.** Replying "we appreciate the offer but X's fee range starts at €2,500" is a voice that belongs to the booker. Canned templates are rejected by experienced agents because promoters recognise them.
- **Relationship memory.** The note "Marta at Sala Apolo was at Primavera Pro 2024, prefers WhatsApp, hates long email threads, has booked us 4 times" is a human capability assisted by software, not replaced by it. The tool should carry the note; it should not auto-generate it.
- **Artist-trust substitute.** The artist's relationship is with the human booker, not with a tool. If the tool becomes the interface, the artist leaves. This is the single strongest constraint on Hour's UX for this profile.
