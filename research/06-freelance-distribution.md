# Profile 06 — Freelance cultural distributor / independent booking agent

A researcher's briefing for the Hour product team. This profile is not glamorous, and it is under-documented in English. Most of the primary source material lives in Catalan, Spanish, and French — in association guides, sector manuals, and long-form interviews in cultural press. Wherever the sources are thin, this document says so.

The profile matters to Hour beyond its raw headcount. It is the archetype of the **multi-tenant user**: one human being operating in parallel inside several companies' universes, with partial visibility, partial loyalty, and a private layer that outlasts any single client. If Hour's data model cannot handle this person comfortably, the product is philosophically broken. Every design decision in sections 8, 10, and 11 should be read with that in mind.

---

## 1. What they juggle

A freelance cultural distributor in Spain, Catalonia, or France typically represents between **three and eight companies or artists** at any given time. Practitioners consulted through the ADGAE and CDAEC orbit confirm that beyond four to five active companies the quality of the relationship with programmers and with the companies themselves starts to deteriorate — there is only so much personal attention one person can give. The distribuidora is not a sales rep moving catalogue items; she is a relational professional, and relationships do not scale linearly.

The typical shape of a portfolio:

- **3-5 active companies** being sold right now (the "core portfolio").
- **1-3 companies in a lighter relationship** — retainer paused, only the occasional gig.
- **Dormant companies**: ex-clients, seasonal clients, or companies where a specific show is still selling but the relationship otherwise ended.
- Each active company has typically **2-5 shows in the current catalogue**: one recent premiere still touring, one "warhorse" (a show that has been touring for years, the company's cash cow), sometimes a small-format piece sold to schools or smaller venues, sometimes a co-produced show with a partner company. At Baro d'evel's scale, for example, the company is actively touring *Qui som?* alongside earlier repertoire — and this "catalogue breadth" is typical even at smaller companies (Festival d'Avignon, 2025).

Totals: **between 15 and 40 "sellable products"** in circulation at any given moment across the portfolio. Each product has its own technical requirements, its own price band, its own target venue type (sala, carpa, carrer, festival).

The contact book sits around **500-2000 programmers**, cumulatively, built over 10-25 years of career. In Spain and Catalonia the sector is small enough that most active programmers are personally known. In France, Germany, the Netherlands, and Belgium the freelance's reach is narrower and depends on each fair season to refresh it. The contact book is **shared-but-separated across clients** — the freelance knows everyone, but a given programmer may only have been pitched on two of the five companies the freelance represents.

This is anecdotal but widely attested across interviews in *entreacte.cat* and *nuvol.com*: the freelance's portfolio is almost never chosen through a neat strategy. It accretes. A company they worked with at a previous job, a friend's band, a director they admire, a referral from another distribuidora when she hit capacity. Marta Oliveres's own trajectory — starting as road manager for Comediants, then working with La Fura dels Baus, Carles Santos, eventually building MOM (Marta Oliveres Management) and El Vivero — is the exemplary Catalan case (ca.wikipedia.org; nuvol.com).

## 2. Annual cycle

Freelance distributors live inside the fair calendar. The European circuit dictates the year much more than any single company's premiere schedule.

**September-December — selling for next season.** The autumn fair cluster: FiraTàrrega (street arts, Lleida, 10-13 September 2026 for the upcoming edition; firatarrega.cat), Mercat de Música Viva de Vic, Fira Mediterrània de Manresa, plus regional Spanish fairs (Huesca, Tárrega, Donostia). This is the peak sales window for the following season, because programmers for municipal and regional theatres confirm their programming for the January-June semester in October-December.

**January-April — selling summer festivals and international dates.** FETEN Gijón (22-27 February 2026 for the children/family fair; makma.net), Feria de Valladolid, CINARS when it happens (biennial, Montreal, 9-14 November 2026; cinars.org), IETM plenary and satellite meetings (ietm.org), Aerowaves Spring Forward (aerowaves.org). Programmers for summer festivals confirm in this window.

**May-July — touring production, fewer sales.** The freelance shifts from sales mode to advance mode: riders, hospitality, travel, invoicing. This is the "execution quarter" when the previous year's sales become reality.

**August — collapse.** Almost everyone takes some holiday. A minority of distributors who work with street-arts companies are on the road through August (FiraTàrrega prep, Chalon dans la Rue, etc.).

**Pre-fair preparation** is a brutal multi-client workload: for each company, an updated catálogo, updated dossier, updated video reel, availability grid, meeting schedule for the fair itself. The peak workload falls three to four weeks before each major fair — a Tanzmesse or CINARS cycle can mean 20-40 scheduled professional meetings in four days, split across multiple companies. (Note: the internationale tanzmesse nrw **has been cancelled for 2026** after the North Rhine-Westphalia Ministry of Culture withdrew funding for the edition; the next live event is projected for 2028. Online sessions are being programmed as a bridge — tanzmeeting.de.)

**Post-fair follow-up** is the other peak: roughly **six weeks of systematic email follow-ups** to every programmer met at the fair. Industry folklore puts the conversion rate for fair meetings at roughly 1 confirmed gig per 30-50 first-contact interactions across the portfolio, though this varies wildly by company profile and freelance experience. No solid published data on this ratio — figure is anecdotal, from ADGAE and CDAEC members.

## 3. Difusión / booking

The freelance's pitch is fundamentally **"I represent [company X]"**, not "here is my curated roster". This is a subtle but consequential distinction from the manager/booking-agent profile (see Profile 08). The distribuidora is, legally and commercially, the voice of the company to the programmer. She is not the impresario. She does not own the show.

Day-to-day selling activity:

- **Cold email and targeted outreach** — tailored per company, per show, per venue. A good distribuidora will not blast. She will pitch one show to one programmer at a time, referencing something specific about that programmer's past programming. This is a hand-crafted discipline; the volume of Gmail drafts written and abandoned is its own productivity problem.
- **Referrals and warm introductions** between programmers, other distributors, and institutional allies (INAEM, ICEC, Pro Helvetia when a Swiss partner is involved).
- **Fair meetings** — 15-30 minute speed-networking sessions, often with barely time between slots to update notes. Note-taking for these sessions is a known pain point: the freelance takes paper notes, sometimes photographs a business card, often loses context by the third day.
- **Dossiers, videos, teaser trailers** — every company has its own dossier (a design-heavy PDF) for each show. The freelance's job includes pressuring the company to keep the dossier up to date.

The **sales cycle for a live-arts show** is slow relative to most B2B: dossier → video → programmer sees the show live at a fair or in a "repesca" tour → programmer issues a "carta de interés" / letter of intent → draft contract → signed contract, typically 9-18 months before the performance date. A gig confirmed at FiraTàrrega 2026 September will often play in spring 2027. The freelance manages this long pipeline across every company simultaneously.

A subtle but important pattern noted in ADGAE's 2023 publication *La distribución de las artes escénicas en España* by Eva Moraga Guerrero (adgae.org): the same programmer will often receive pitches from the same freelance for **multiple shows from multiple companies**. This is operationally a minefield — the programmer does not remember which show came from which company, the freelance mis-files replies, and the follow-up gets tangled across client files.

## 4. Production

The freelance **rarely handles production**. That work belongs to the company's production manager (when the company has one) or to the company's administration office (when it does not).

What the freelance *does* handle in the production phase:

- **The communication channel** between programmer and company once a gig is confirmed. She forwards, translates, mediates, chases.
- **The advance** — hospitality, arrival times, meal preferences, dressing rooms, travel logistics — when the company has no dedicated production manager. In the Spanish/Catalan scene, many small companies do not have one, so the distribuidora de facto doubles as advance coordinator for six weeks before the show.
- **Rider checking**: does the venue's stage match the company's ficha técnica? The freelance forwards riders and pushes back on venue objections.

This is the most under-estimated part of the job. The freelance spends 30-40 percent of her time on work that is not selling — it is operational follow-through on the gigs she already sold, months ago. This is why a raw "leads pipeline" CRM misses the point: the work continues long after the deal closes.

## 5. Archive and materials

This is where the multi-client problem hits hardest. The freelance needs constant, fast, up-to-date access to each company's:

- **Dossier PDF** (often in 2-3 languages, updated every 6-12 months)
- **Show video** (full-length private link + 2-5 minute promo trailer)
- **Ficha técnica / technical rider** (updated whenever the company changes a lighting design or adds a set piece)
- **High-resolution photos** (with photographer credits, often contested)
- **Press dossier** (reviews, articles, podcast mentions)
- **Contract templates** (each company has its own preferred model)
- **Biography and CV files** for the artists
- **Logo + branding assets** in multiple formats

Multiply that by five companies and you get 30-50 critical files that must never be out of date, must always be findable, and must never be mixed up. Distribuidoras consulted through APGCC's *Programa de Capacitació de Distribuïdores* (gestiocultural.org) consistently describe this as "the folder nightmare" — a personal master index, usually in a Google Sheet or Notion page, with every company's latest asset URL.

The **"updated rider problem"** multiplied by N clients is the signature pain. One distribuidora interviewed for APCC's *Guia 4: Distribució* (apcc.cat) described sending a six-month-old version of a rider to a programmer, who forwarded it to the venue's technical director, who built a plot based on it — and only at the advance call two weeks before the show did everyone realise the rider was stale. Rebuilding the plot cost the company a discount off its fee.

## 6. Money

Three income patterns, usually combined:

- **Retainer** — a monthly fee paid by each client company, typically €800-2500/mes depending on how active the company is and the freelance's seniority. The retainer buys "coverage" — the freelance being available, representing, showing up at fairs. This figure is the best available industry estimate, synthesised from anecdotal reports on APCC's *Guia 4: Distribució* and from direct conversations inside the CDAEC network. No published rate card exists.
- **Commission on confirmed gigs** — typically **10-20 percent of the net cachet** of each booking. The commission is paid by the company, not by the venue. Standard payment structure: 50 percent at confirmation, 50 percent after the performance.
- **Occasional project fees** — for a specific international tour, a specific fair attendance, a specific residency application.

The freelance does **not** invoice the programmer or the venue directly. That is the company's job. This is a critical boundary: the freelance is never the contracting party for the gig itself. She is a service provider to the company.

The legal status in Spain is almost always **autónoma**. She registers her activity under an IAE code in the cultural/services band (usually 844 or 899), issues invoices with 21 percent IVA to her client companies (the 10 percent reduced IVA for live-performance services applies to the artist-to-organizer relationship, not to the distribuidora-to-company relationship; sympathyforthelawyer.com, supercontable.com). She pays quarterly VAT (modelo 303), quarterly IRPF, and annual resumen (modelo 390, modelo 130). She files income from multiple clients — which makes her a textbook "autónoma pluriactividad" case in the eyes of the Agencia Tributaria.

SGAE collection is **not her problem**. Authors' rights are settled directly between SGAE and the venue.

In France the picture is slightly different. A *diffuseur indépendant* often operates through a *bureau de production* structure (association or SARL) which can employ the diffuseur, invoice on her behalf, and sometimes even employ the artists on short contracts (CDDU). The French bureau-de-production model blurs the pure-freelance figure — see La Magnanerie, Plató, Akompani (magnanerie-spectacle.com, bureauplato.com, akompani.fr) for real examples. Hour should not assume the Spanish freelance pattern everywhere in Europe.

## 7. Tools today and why they fail

This section is the root-cause of Hour's product opportunity. The freelance stack today, by consensus:

- **Gmail** — very often **4 Gmail addresses**, one per client company, or one personal address with aggressive filters and labels per company. This is not a quirk; it is a deliberate privacy-partitioning move. Programmers expect to reach "the person for Company X" at an address that belongs to Company X, not at a shared inbox.
- **Google Drive** — separate top-level folder per client, with a strict folder hierarchy: `/Dossiers`, `/Video`, `/Fichas técnicas`, `/Fotos`, `/Prensa`, `/Contratos`.
- **Google Sheets or Excel** — the master tracker. Usually one sheet per client company (*Tracking Girà 2026*), plus a personal **meta-tracker** the freelance keeps for herself that spans all her clients. The meta-tracker is the *real* product of a freelance distributor's knowledge and the one artifact she will not hand over to anyone.
- **Airtable** — repeatedly tried, repeatedly abandoned. Airtable is the "promised land" — programmable, relational, pretty. But the maintenance cost is high, and the collapse pattern is always the same: the freelance builds the schema, uses it for six months, falls behind during a peak fair season, and never catches up. The community forum evidence on this (community.airtable.com jobs-board threads 45434 and 41361) is telling: freelance music supervisors and distributors are actively looking to *hire* Airtable consultants because the DIY approach is not sustainable.
- **Notion** — tried, usually abandoned for the same reason, plus its weak calendar and timeline capabilities for fair planning.
- **Sector software** (Artifax, ArtsVision, Propared, Eventotron) — rarely adopted. These tools are built for venue managers and large producing theatres. Artifax Event targets "event planning, room hire, resource scheduling, finances, artistic and production schedules, education bookings and tour scheduling" for a venue or a large organization (artifax.com). ArtsVision is a cloud-based tool with shared data pools for large performing-arts organisations (site.artsvision.net). Neither is built for a freelance straddling five clients with separate data boundaries. Propared is closer to the production-side world, but again organization-first. Pricing is also a barrier — most of these tools are priced for institutions, not for a person with €800-2500/mes per client.
- **Music-side tools** (Gigwell, Overture) — exist and are mature for booking agencies in music (gigwell.com, bookingwithoverture.com), but they assume a single-agency-single-roster data model. The multi-client freelance is not their customer.

The fundamental mismatch: **every sector tool is built around a single organisation's internal team.** A freelance who lives across five organisations cannot be modelled inside any of them without either (a) giving each client access to everything, which she will never accept, or (b) maintaining five separate accounts, which defeats the point.

## 8. Collaboration patterns — the core tension

This is the hardest product question Hour will face, and it deserves to be named plainly.

**Whose contact book is it?**

The freelance's answer: **mine**. She built it over a decade or two. The programmer at Théâtre de la Bastille who always picks up when she calls has known her for eight years across three different client companies. The relationship outlasts any individual contract.

**The company's answer: ours.** Every gig the freelance sells on our behalf, every programmer we meet through her, every deal, every invoice — that is business we are generating and paying her a commission to close. When she leaves, we expect to inherit the relationship.

This is the same legal dispute that runs through insurance brokerage, real estate, and travel agency work (agencyconsulting.com, ncrealtors.org, janinelovestravel.co.uk): who owns the book of business when the agent leaves? In Spain and France there is no standard contractual template for performing-arts distributors on this question. Each freelance-company contract handles it differently, and many handle it not at all — until the break-up.

**How freelances currently protect themselves:**

- **Separate Gmails per client** so each client's correspondence stays inside that client's mailbox (forensically ownable by the client if needed) while the *freelance's own relational layer* — her notes, her meta-tracker, her WhatsApps with programmers — stays outside all client infrastructure.
- **Separate Drives per client** with the client as owner of their folder.
- **A personal meta-CRM outside any client** — usually a Google Sheet, sometimes Airtable, sometimes just a Moleskine. This is where she keeps: "Programmer X at Venue Y, met him in 2018, interested in physical theatre, has a kid, speaks Catalan, do not pitch before 11am, last contact Oct 2025". This layer does not belong to any client. It is the freelance's personal IP.
- **When a client leaves**, the freelance keeps the relationships, the client keeps the fees and the formal contact-of-record email chain.

**Implications for Hour's design (stated as requirements, not suggestions):**

1. The product must support a **freelance workspace** that is *distinct* from any client's organisation workspace, and that can hold the freelance's private contact layer.
2. A freelance-user must be able to operate *inside* a client's organisation as a role-scoped member, seeing and editing only what that organisation has shared with her.
3. The freelance must be able to log a contact in her own private workspace, and *optionally* share it (or a subset of its fields) with a specific client organisation — but the default must be private.
4. Leaving a client must not export the freelance's private layer. Neither must it strip the client of the correspondence, contracts, and gig records that belong to that organisation.
5. The product must model the **long-lived human relationship** (programmer X) separately from the **instance of engagement** (programmer X <-> Company A, programmer X <-> Company B). The same programmer can be a contact inside multiple organisations *and* a private contact in the freelance's own layer, with different notes and different histories in each context.

This is not a feature. This is the architecture.

## 9. Real vocabulary

A freelance distributor in Barcelona routinely moves between four working languages in a single week. Vocabulary distortion between them is a real, daily source of confusion. Terms are preserved here in the language of use.

| freelance (in use) | freelance (in use) | freelance | indépendant·e |

Three vocabulary traps worth flagging for any non-specialist designer working on Hour:

1. **"Difusión" in music ≠ "distribución" in theatre/dance.** In the music industry "difusión" means getting the record played on radio/playlists; the person who books the live gigs is the "booking agent" or "mánager de directo". In theatre/dance "distribución" (ES) / "distribució" (CA) / "diffusion" (FR) *is* the live-gigs-booking function. Hour will serve both worlds — do not let a music-industry team member accidentally mistranslate the theatre concept.
2. **"Catalan distribuïdora" vs. "Spanish distribuidora"** — the word is essentially the same, but the communities are distinct (CDAEC in Catalonia, created 2020, holacdaec.wixsite.com; ADGAE at national Spanish level, twenty years old, adgae.org). A freelance in Barcelona routinely moves between both. The data model should not assume one nationality = one association.
3. **"Bureau de production" (FR) ≠ "distribuidora" (ES).** The French bureau handles production, administration, AND distribution; the Spanish distribuidora typically only does distribution. Hour must not collapse them into one profile.

## 10. Friction stories

These stories are composite and anonymised, drawn from interviews referenced in CDAEC, ADGAE, and APGCC materials, and from APCC's pedagogical *Guia 4: Distribució* (apcc.cat). They are flagged explicitly as illustrative; some details are reconstructed.

**The four-Gmail fair meeting.** A distribuidora sits down at Tanzmesse for a 20-minute meeting. The programmer says "You pitched me something last month — was it the circus piece or the dance piece?" She has no idea, because the pitch went from one of her four Gmails. She guesses the wrong one. The meeting pivots awkwardly. Fifteen minutes of oxygen consumed recovering.

**The wrong-folder rider.** A programmer asks for the rider of *Company A's* show. The distribuidora, in a hurry between a train and a phone call, opens Drive, clicks the file that says "ficha_técnica_v3.pdf", and attaches it. It belonged to Company B's show — which has three times the lighting channels and a circular dance floor. The programmer gives it to the venue. Three weeks before the show, during advance, the technical director points out that the rider makes no sense. The distribuidora eats the embarrassment and sends the correct file, apologising to both companies.

**The tangled thread.** A programmer emails the distribuidora asking about two shows — one from Company A, one from Company C. The distribuidora replies from one of her Gmails. Company A's thread now contains details about Company C. She has to manually copy-paste between inboxes, redact, forward. The mental overhead is enormous. By the next exchange the thread has bifurcated: she is now replying to two versions of the same conversation, and the programmer starts to feel she is being disorganised.

**The holiday dates.** The distribuidora goes away for two weeks in late August. Company B's production manager urgently needs to know whether the 14 November gig in Bordeaux is confirmed. It is — but the confirmation email sits inside the distribuidora's personal Gmail, not inside any shared folder. The company cannot see it. The production manager emails the programmer directly. The programmer is confused about who the point of contact is. By the time the distribuidora is back, the relationship has a small scratch on it.

**The break-up.** A company terminates its relationship with the distribuidora after three years. The company demands "the full contact history" — every programmer ever pitched, every reply, every note. The distribuidora feels ambushed: she considers most of those programmers her personal network. There is no written contract clause to settle it. A messy email exchange follows. The distribuidora ends up handing over the email chains that mention the company, but keeps her notes, her personal WhatsApp conversations, her calendar entries with programmers, and her private meta-tracker. The company gets the paper trail; the distribuidora keeps the relationships. Neither side is fully happy.

**The forgotten follow-up.** Six weeks after Fira Mediterrània, the distribuidora scans her post-fair checklist. She has followed up with 23 of the 34 programmers she met. The 11 missed ones were logged in her personal meta-sheet, not in the per-company trackers, because they were met in mixed contexts. She catches eight of them in time. Three are now too late — the programming window closed. One of the three was *the* breakthrough opportunity for Company D in Germany.

Each of these stories points at the same underlying structural flaw: **the tools scatter the data around the edges of her workflow**. Nothing is designed for the freelance as the primary unit.

## 11. What they do NOT want software to do

This section is non-negotiable for Hour. It came up unprompted in every interview referenced, across CDAEC, ADGAE, APGCC, and French SYNAVI materials (synavi.org).

- **Do not build a "global roster" that mixes all clients.** The freelance does not want Hour to present her five companies to the world as if they were a single agency roster. That is not her product. She represents each of them separately, and the companies themselves would be furious at being co-branded against each other.
- **Do not share her contact book by default.** Her value is her network. Any feature that silently replicates her contacts into a client organisation's address book — "helpfully", "for convenience" — is existential-threat-level. The contact book must be opt-in to share at the contact level, not the default.
- **Do not let algorithms "suggest" contacts between companies.** A programmer the freelance knows personally is not a piece of shared infrastructure. Hour should never prompt Company A with "by the way, your distribuidora also knows programmer X — want to reach out?".
- **Do not conflate her private notes with shared notes.** When she writes "this programmer hates cold pitches, only responds to referrals" in her private layer, it must never leak into any client organisation's view.
- **Do not gate her work on the company's login.** If a client company goes dormant, disables their Hour subscription, or leaves, the freelance must still be able to access *her* layer — her contacts, her meta-tracker, her history. She will only adopt Hour if it continues to serve her even when a client churns.
- **Do not enforce a collaboration model that pushes her into "one workspace per client".** She is willing to work inside a client's Hour organisation when it makes sense, but she needs her own umbrella view to exist in parallel.

The positive framing of the same principles: **her world, her layer, her rules — with explicit sharing surfaces into each client's world**. This is the Hour multi-tenancy sketch for this profile.

## 12. Sector references

Primary institutional bodies and sources consulted or cited above:

- **ADGAE — Asociación de Empresas de Distribución y Gestión de las Artes Escénicas** (adgae.org). National Spanish association, 20+ years old, publishes sector studies. 2023 publication *La distribución de las artes escénicas en España* by Eva Moraga Guerrero is the most authoritative recent text.
- **CDAEC — Col·lectiu de Distribuïdores d'Arts Escèniques de Catalunya** (holacdaec.wixsite.com/cdaec; @cdaec1 on X). Created 2020, Catalan-specific, union-like advocacy body. Most freelance distribuidoras in Catalonia are members.
- **ADVAEM** (advaem.es) — Valencian counterpart. Smaller but active, covers both performing arts and music.
- **APCC — Associació Professional de Circ de Catalunya** (apcc.cat). Publishes pedagogical guides including *Guia 4: La distribució d'espectacles*, a practical training document directly relevant to this profile.
- **APGCC — Associació de Professionals de la Gestió Cultural de Catalunya** (gestiocultural.org). Runs the *Programa de Capacitació de Distribuïdores*, whose module materials describe the distribuidora's daily practice.
- **Xarxa de Productores** (xarxaproductores.cat). Training formatives include practitioners like Marta Oliveres.
- **SYNAVI — Syndicat national des arts vivants** (synavi.org). French sector union, represents independent arts teams including bureaus de production and diffuseurs.
- **ARTCENA** (artcena.fr). French national center for circus, street arts and theatre, publishes the *Se structurer* guide that explains bureau-de-production, administration, and diffusion roles.
- **IETM — International Network for Contemporary Performing Arts** (ietm.org). Plenary meetings and Artists Working Group are primary networking venues for international distribution activity; freelance distributors can be individual members.
- **Aerowaves** (aerowaves.org). Dance-specific discovery network across 34 countries; its Twenty programme generates ~100 performance opportunities a year and involves freelance distribuidoras as gatekeepers.
- **CINARS Biennale Montreal** (cinars.org). Biennial, next 9-14 November 2026. ~1500 professionals, ~330 programmers — a standard freelance anchor event for international touring.
- **FiraTàrrega** (firatarrega.cat). 10-13 September 2026. The single most important annual fair for Catalan street-arts distribution.
- **FETEN Gijón** (descubregijon.com/en/feten). 22-27 February 2026. The peak fair for children/family performing arts distribution in Spain.
- **On The Move** (on-the-move.org). European cultural mobility information network; its yearbooks and resource library document funding flows and the infrastructure around cross-border distribution.
- **Circostrada** (circostrada.org). European circus and street-arts network; its publications address distribution and mobility in those disciplines.

Individual figures worth reading through for this profile:

- **Marta Oliveres** (Barcelona). Founder of MOM / El Vivero. Interviews in *nuvol.com* (2020-2024) document her trajectory from road manager with Comediants to senior distribuidora. Canonical case study.
- **Elclimamola** — Marine Budin and Marcela Imazio (Barcelona, Calle Salvà 45). Contemporary-dance-focused distribution agency. Public profile via elclimamola.com and redescena.net.
- **Palosanto Projects** (Barcelona). National and international distribution with a contemporary-dance roster (Luz Arcas / La Pharmaco, Delreves Danza Vertical, Jose y sus hermanas). Example of a small-team distribution structure between pure freelance and full agency (palosantoprojects.com).
- **Art Republic** — Elise Garriga, Iva Horvat (Castelldefels). International-touring strategy for artists like Agrupación Señor Serrano, La Tristura, Aurora Bauzà y Pere Jou (artrepublic.es).
- **La Magnanerie, Plató, Akompani** (France). Examples of the French bureau-de-production model that the Spanish freelance pattern contrasts against (magnanerie-spectacle.com, bureauplato.com, akompani.fr).
